'''Tom House''' (born 1949 in [[Durham, North Carolina]]) is an American singer-songwriter and poet whose music combines elements of [[country music|country]], singer-songwriter, and [[folk music|folk]].<ref name=exclaim>{{cite web | url=http://exclaim.ca/music/article/tom_house-winding_down_road | title=Winding Down The Road Review | work=[[Exclaim!]] | date=July 2, 2012 | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Schneider, Jason}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.allmusic.com/artist/tom-house-mn0000931811/biography | title=Tom House Biography | website=[[AllMusic]] | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Unterberger, Richie}}</ref><ref name=tears>{{cite web | url=http://nodepression.com/article/tom-house-poets-tears-drunken-smile | title=Tom House – A poet's tears, a drunken smile | work=[[No Depression (magazine)|No Depression]] | date=April 30, 1997 | accessdate=June 24, 2017 | author=Warren, Bill Friskics}}</ref>

==Biography==
House wrote hundreds of poems during the two decades before 1997. Of these, three had been included in ''The Bicentennial Edition of the Tennessee Anthology of Poetry''.<ref name=scene>{{cite web | url=http://www.nashvillescene.com/arts-culture/article/13001582/local-poetsongwriter-tom-houses-new-cd-captures-his-deep-tales-and-offbeat-cadences | title=Local poet/songwriter Tom House's new CD captures his deep tales and offbeat cadences | work=[[Nashville Scene]] | date=July 24, 1997 | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Friskics-Warren, Bill}}</ref> From 1982 to 1988, he edited and published the journal ''raw bone'', which was known for publishing spare, brutal writing.<ref name=tears/> House's first recording to be released was "The Hank Williams Memorial Myth", a spoken-word intro to the 1996 compilation album ''Nashville: The Other Side Of The Alley''.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://nodepression.com/article/tom-house-welcome-occupation | title=Tom House – Welcome to the occupation | work=No Depression | date=December 31, 2001 | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Alden, Grant}}</ref> In 1997, he released his debut album, ''The Neighborhood Is Changing'', on [[Checkered Past Records]].<ref>{{cite web | url=http://countrystandardtime.com/d/cdreview.asp?xid=2735 | title=The Neighborhood Is Changing | work=[[Country Standard Time]] | accessdate=June 24, 2017 | author=Sacks, Bill}}</ref> The album featured multiple members of [[Lambchop (band)|Lambchop]].<ref>{{cite book | url=https://archive.org/details/musicusaroughgui0000unte | url-access=registration | title=Music USA: The Rough Guide | publisher=[[Rough Guides]] | author=Unterberger, Ritchie | year=1999 | pages=[https://archive.org/details/musicusaroughgui0000unte/page/121 121]}}</ref> As of 2012, he had released a total of 13 albums.<ref name=exclaim/>

==Critical reception==
[[Greil Marcus]] wrote in ''[[Esquire (magazine)|Esquire]]'' that House's 1998 album ''This White Man's Burden'' was "an extraordinary collection of warnings and threats, and it sounds as if it came right out of the ground."<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.mysongwriters.com/TomHouse/TH_esquire.htm | title=Devil's Music | work=Esquire | date=December 1998 | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Marcus, Greil}}</ref> He later named the album his 6th favorite of 1998.<ref>{{cite web | url=http://www.mtv.com/news/510258/critics-picks-greil-marcus-top-10/ | title=Greil Marcus' Top 10 | publisher=[[MTV News]] | date=December 18, 1998 | accessdate=June 24, 2017 | author=Staff}}{{dead link|date=June 2024|bot=medic}}{{cbignore|bot=medic}}</ref> [[Robert Christgau]], however, was less favorable in his review of the album, awarding it a "neither" rating, indicating that it "may impress once or twice with consistent craft or an arresting track or two. Then it won't."<ref>{{cite book | title=Christgau's Consumer Guide: Albums of the '90s | publisher=[[Macmillan Publishing]] | author=Christgau, Robert | year=2000 | pages=137 | chapter=CG: Tom House | chapter-url=http://robertchristgau.com/get_artist.php?name=tom+house}}</ref><ref>{{cite web | url=http://robertchristgau.com/xg/bk-cg90/grades-90s.php | title=Key to Icons | work=Robertchristgau.com | accessdate=June 24, 2017}}</ref> Jim Caligiuri of the ''[[Austin Chronicle]]'' wrote that House's third album, ''{{'}}Til You've Seen Mine'', was "easily his most accomplished" and gave it 3 out of 4 stars.<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.austinchronicle.com/music/2000-03-17/76380/ | title=Tom House 'Til You've Seen Mine (Catamount) | work=Austin Chronicle | date=March 17, 2000 | accessdate=June 23, 2017 | author=Caligiuri, Jim}}</ref> [[Erik Hage]] of ''[[No Depression (magazine)|No Depression]]'' wrote that on House's 2004 album ''That Dark Calling'', "House is still very much his own man, but there's a levity to his approach here that suggests the singer is perhaps more concerned with healing than drumming up haints and spooks."<ref>{{cite web | url=http://nodepression.com/album-review/tom-house-dark-calling | title=Tom House – That Dark Calling | work=No Depression | date=February 29, 2004 | accessdate=June 24, 2017 | author=Hage, Erik}}</ref> Andy Whitman of ''[[Paste (magazine)|Paste]]'' wrote that on the album, "House is a fine songwriter with a great eye for detail, but his dour sensibilities become oppressive after a while."<ref>{{cite web | url=https://www.pastemagazine.com/articles/2004/08/tom-house-that-dark-calling.html | title=Tom House – That Dark Calling | work=[[Paste (magazine)|Paste]] | date=August 1, 2004 | accessdate=June 24, 2017 | author=Whitman, Andy}}</ref>
