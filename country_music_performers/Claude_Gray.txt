'''Claude Gray Jr.''' (January 25, 1932 – April 28, 2023), nicknamed "'''The Tall Texan'''", was an American [[country music]] singer-songwriter and guitar picker best known for his 1960 hit "[[Family Bible (song)|Family Bible]]", which has been covered by many different artists. Gray's other hit, "I'll Just Have a Cup of Coffee (Then I'll Go)", was covered and rearranged by [[Jamaicans|Jamaican]] [[reggae]] singer-songwriter [[Bob Marley]], who retitled the song as "One Cup Of Coffee".

Alongside artists such as [[Ray Price (musician)|Ray Price]], [[Jim Reeves]], [[Eddy Arnold]], [[Don Gibson]] and [[Chet Atkins]], Gray was a purveyor of the [[Nashville sound]], embracing the [[Countrypolitan]] movement which paved the way for pop-oriented singers in country music and attracted new audiences to the genre.

==Early life==
Gray was born in [[Henderson, Texas]], on January 25, 1932,<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.foxnews.com/wires/2009Jan25/0,4670,History,00.html |title=Today in History – Jan. 25 |publisher=[[Fox News Channel]] |date=January 25, 2009}}</ref><ref name="LarkinGE">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Guinness Encyclopedia of Popular Music]]|editor=[[Colin Larkin (writer)|Colin Larkin]]|publisher=[[Guinness Publishing]]|date=1992|edition=First|isbn=0-85112-939-0|page=1019}}</ref> where he started his singing career while attending high school.<ref name="LarkinGE"/> After school, he served in the [[United States Navy]] from 1950 to 1954. Upon his return to home, he worked as a salesman for the rest of the decade. He began a recording career in 1959, after working as a radio announcer in [[Kilgore, Texas]], and performing as a disc jockey in [[Meridian, Mississippi]].<ref name="all">{{cite web |url= {{AllMusic|class=artist|id=p24363/biography|pure_url=yes}} |title=Claude Gray |last=Brennan |first=Sandra |website=[[AllMusic]] }}</ref>

==Career==
Gray was approached by D Records in 1959, and recorded "I'm Not Supposed", which was released as his first single. The song made the Cashbox country charts.<ref name="all"/> The following year, Gray and two friends purchased the song "[[Family Bible (song)|Family Bible]]" from [[Willie Nelson]]<ref name="Mohave">{{Cite news |url=https://news.google.com/newspapers?id=OX0LAAAAIBAJ&sjid=eVMDAAAAIBAJ&pg=5213,4246602&dq=claude-gray |title=Country music show to be one-night stand |first=Jo |last=Gray |newspaper=Mohave Daily Miner |date=June 7, 1988 |postscript=<!--None--> }}</ref> for $100.<ref>{{cite web |url=https://www.npr.org/templates/story/story.php?storyId=90066047 |first=Ed |last=Ward |title=Willie Nelson: The Songwriter at 75 |publisher=NPR |date=April 30, 2008 }}</ref> Gray then recorded the song, and released it as a single.<ref name="all"/> It peaked at No.&nbsp;10 on the country chart.<ref name="LarkinGE"/> In 1961, "I'll Just Have a Cup of Coffee (Then I'll Go)", was released, which peaked at No.&nbsp;4, and was followed by the biggest hit of Gray's career, the No.&nbsp;3 "My Ears Should Burn (When Fools Are Talked About)", which was penned by [[Roger Miller]].<ref name="LarkinGE"/> Gray's final top ten hit came in 1967 with "I Never Had the One I Wanted", which was also his final charted song upon re-release in 1979.<ref name="chart">{{cite web |url={{AllMusic|class=artist|id=p24363/charts-awards/billboard-singles|pure_url=yes}} |title=Claude Gray > Charts & Awards > Billboard Singles |publisher=[[AllMusic]] }}</ref>

In the late 1970s, Gray co-wrote with Walt Breeland, a controversial song titled "The Ballad of Jimmy Hoffa". Gray's record label at the time ([[Mercury Records]]) refused to allow him to record the song, but he eventually recorded it for Ol' Podner Records, who subsequently released it directly to the [[Teamsters]].<ref name="all"/> In 1986, he released a cover of [[Neil Diamond]]'s "[[Sweet Caroline]]".<ref name="all"/>

Gray continued to tour with The Claude Gray Roadshow, performing shows throughout North America and in parts of Europe, where classic country music remains popular.{{citation needed|date=April 2023}}

==Personal life and death==
Gray was {{convert|6|ft|5|in|m|2}} and because of his height, was given the moniker, "The Tall Texan."<ref name="all"/>

On April 18, 2023, a social media post shared by a Gray family friend, stated that Gray had entered [[hospice]] care after the discovery of a large tumor on his brain, leaving him non-cognitive.<ref>{{Cite web |title=My Kind Of Country Music (Radio Station) |url=https://www.facebook.com/login/?next=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.facebook.com%2Fmykindofcountrymusic |access-date=2023-04-18 |website=Facebook |language=en}}</ref> He died in [[Skokie, Illinois]], on April 28, 2023, at the age of 91.<ref name="Crawford obit">{{cite web |title=Mr. Claude Gray Jr.|url=https://www.crawfordacrim.com/obituaries/Claude-Gray-2/#!/Obituary |website=Crawford-A.Crim Funeral Home|access-date=April 29, 2023|date=April 29, 2023}}</ref><ref name="obiSCM">{{cite web |url=https://www.savingcountrymusic.com/family-bible-singer-the-tall-texan-claude-gray-has-died/|title="Family Bible" Singer, 'The Tall Texan' Claude Gray Has Died|date=April 30, 2023|access-date=May 4, 2023|website=SavingCountryMusic.com}}</ref>
