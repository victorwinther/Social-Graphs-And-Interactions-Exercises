'''Calvin Grant Shofner''' (April 7, 1932 – October 10, 2013),<ref name="Obit"/> known professionally as '''Cal Smith''', was an American [[country music|country]] [[musician]], most famous for his 1974 hits "[[Country Bumpkin]]" and "[[It's Time to Pay the Fiddler]]".

==Career==
Calvin Grant Shofner was born on April 7, 1932, in [[Gans, Oklahoma]],<ref name="whitburn">{{cite book|last=Whitburn|first=Joel|title=Hot Country Songs 1944 to 2008|publisher=Record Research, Inc|date=2008|page=385|isbn=978-0-89820-177-2}}</ref><ref name=billboard>{{cite news|last=Dauphin|first=Chuck|title='Country Bumpkin' Singer Cal Smith Dies at 81|url=http://www.billboard.com/articles/columns/the-615/5755310/country-bumpkin-singer-cal-smith-dies-at-81|accessdate=April 21, 2015|newspaper=[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]|date=October 11, 2013}}</ref><ref name=tennessean>{{cite web |url=http://blogs.tennessean.com/tunein/2013/10/10/cal-smith-voice-of-country-bumpkin-and-other-hits-dies-at-age-81/ |title=Cal Smith, voice of 'Country Bumpkin' and other hits, dies at age 81 |newspaper=[[The Tennessean]] |accessdate=2013-10-15 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20131014063143/http://blogs.tennessean.com/tunein/2013/10/10/cal-smith-voice-of-country-bumpkin-and-other-hits-dies-at-age-81/ |archive-date=2013-10-14 |url-status=dead }}</ref><ref name=countryweekly>{{cite news|last=Ragusa|first= Tammy|title='Country Bumpkin' Singer Cal Smith Dies at 81|url=http://www.countryweekly.com/news/country-bumpkin-singer-cal-smith-dies|accessdate=April 21, 2015|newspaper=[[Country Weekly]]|date=October 11, 2013}}</ref> as the youngest of three sons of James "Otto" and Ethel (Quinn) Shofner. During the [[Great Depression]], the Smiths headed west and settled in [[Oakland, California|Oakland]], [[California]],<ref name=billboard/> and he grew up in [[San Jose, California|San Jose]], California.<ref name=tennessean/><ref name=countryweekly/><ref name=usatoday>{{cite news|last=Cooper |first=Peter|title=Cal Smith, voice of 'Country Bumpkin,' dies at age 81|url=https://www.usatoday.com/story/life/music/2013/10/11/cal-smith-voice-of-country-bumpkin-dies-at-age-81/2965695/|accessdate=April 21, 2015|newspaper=[[USA Today]]|date=October 11, 2013}}</ref> Smith began his music career performing at the Remember Me Cafe in [[San Francisco]] at the age of 15, but he was not financially successful at first. Throughout the 1950s, he was not able to continue his music career, so he worked at various other jobs, including [[truck driving]] and [[Bronc riding|bronco busting]]. He appeared on the ''[[California Hayride]]'' television show in the mid-1950s before serving two years in the [[United States military|military]].<ref name="allmusic">{{cite web|url=http://www.allmusic.com/artist/cal-smith-mn0000647518/biography|title=Cal Smith biography|last=Manheim|first=James|work=[[Allmusic]]|accessdate=13 October 2013}}</ref>

After his discharge, he began playing in a band in the [[San Francisco Bay Area]]. In 1961, [[country music]] legend [[Ernest Tubb]] heard the band play and, after an audition, hired Smith to play guitar for the [[Texas Troubadours]]. Thus, Smith is heard playing in most of Tubb's 1960s recordings. His first solo single was "Tear Stained Pillow" / "Eleven Long Years" on the local Plaid label. Smith's stage name began to catch on after he released his second solo single, "I'll Just Go Home", in 1966 for [[Kapp Records]], and he first cracked the [[Billboard charts|''Billboard'']] chart with his second single, "The Only Thing I Want".<ref name="whitburn"/>

Smith permanently parted ways with Tubb and the Texas Troubadours in 1969 and he released his first solo album, ''Drinking Champagne'', in 1969.<ref name="LarkinCountry">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Guinness Who's Who of Country Music]]|editor=[[Colin Larkin (writer)|Colin Larkin]]|publisher=[[Guinness Publishing]]|date=1993|edition=First|isbn=0-85112-726-6|pages=377/8}}</ref> The album's title track had reached the Top 40 on the country chart the previous year, and was later a Top 10 hit for [[George Strait]] in 1990.

In 1970, Smith signed with [[Decca Records]], and his popularity quickly soared, starting off with his 1972 Top 10 hit, "[[I've Found Someone of My Own]]".<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> He began recording songs written by some of the biggest names in the industry; for instance, in March 1973, his rendition of [[Bill Anderson (country music)|Bill Anderson's]] "[[The Lord Knows I'm Drinking]]" became his first number-one country hit.<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> When Decca became [[MCA Records]] in 1973, he enjoyed his biggest successes. In 1974, he recorded two of his greatest hits, "[[It's Time to Pay the Fiddler]]" and "[[Country Bumpkin]]",<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> which received Song of the Year Awards from both the [[Academy of Country Music]] and the [[Country Music Association]].

==Later career==
Smith continued to have success with [[MCA Records]] into the late 1970s including the Top 20 singles "Between Lust And Watching TV" (1974), "She Talked A Lot About Texas" (1975), "I Just Came Home To Count The Memories" (1977), and "Come See About Me" (1977).<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> After this he continued to have minor successes that included "The Rise And Fall Of The Roman Empire" in 1979.<ref name="whitburn"/>

Smith released his last album, ''Stories of Life by Cal Smith'', in 1986 on [[Step One Records]],<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> where he scored a minor hit that year with "King Lear".<ref name="whitburn"/>

==Personal life==
In 1977, Smith joined entrepreneur [[Larry Schmittou]] and other country music stars, such as [[Conway Twitty]], [[Jerry Reed]], [[Larry Gatlin]], and [[Richard Sterban]], as investors in the [[Nashville Sounds]], a [[minor league baseball]] team of the [[Double-A (baseball)|Double-A]] [[Southern League (1885–1899)|Southern League]] that began play in 1978.<ref>{{citation|first=Larry|last=Woody|title=Schmittou: A Grand Slam in Baseball, Business, And Life|publisher=Eggmann Publishing Company|location=Nashville|year=1996|isbn=1886371334|pages=64–65}}</ref>

Smith and his wife, Darlene, lived in the [[Branson, Missouri|Branson]], [[Missouri]] area.

Smith died in Branson on October 10, 2013. He is survived by Darlene, his son Calvin, five grandchildren and 15 great-grandchildren. He was preceded in death by a son, Jimmie Todd.<ref name="Obit">{{cite web|title=Country Singer Cal Smith Dies|url=http://www.ozarksfirst.com/story/country-singer-cal-smith-dies/d/story/3ux38oYSdEy5bYAmzhquEA|work=OzarksFirst.com|accessdate=14 October 2013|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20131014154816/http://www.ozarksfirst.com/story/country-singer-cal-smith-dies/d/story/3ux38oYSdEy5bYAmzhquEA|archivedate=14 October 2013}}</ref>
