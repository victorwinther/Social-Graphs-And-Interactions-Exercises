'''Steve Young''' (July 12, 1942 – March 17, 2016)<ref>{{cite news|last1=Betts|first1=Stephen L.|title='Seven Bridges Road' Singer Steve Young Dead at 73|url=https://www.rollingstone.com/music/news/steve-young-dead-died-20160318|access-date=August 30, 2016|agency=Rolling Stone|date=March 18, 2016}}</ref> was an American [[country music]] singer, songwriter and guitarist, known for his song "[[Seven Bridges Road]]" (on ''[[Rock Salt & Nails (album)|Rock Salt & Nails]]'' & ''[[Seven Bridges Road (album)|Seven Bridges Road]]''). He was a pioneer of the [[country rock]], [[Americana (music)|Americana]], and [[alternative country]] sounds, and he was also a vital force behind the [[Outlaw country|outlaw movement]]. 

==Biography==
Born in [[Newnan, Georgia|Newnan]], [[Georgia (U.S. state)|Georgia]], United States,<ref name="LarkinCountry">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Guinness Who's Who of Country Music]]|editor=[[Colin Larkin (writer)|Colin Larkin]]|publisher=[[Guinness Publishing]]|date=1993|edition=First|isbn=0-85112-726-6|pages=472/3}}</ref> he grew up in [[Texas]] and [[Gadsden, Alabama]], moving from place to place as his family looked for work.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://trevormcshanemusic.wordpress.com/2010/07/15/steveyoung/ |title=Interview with singer, songwriter and musician: Steve Young &#124; The Adventures of Trevor McShane |website=Trevormcshanemusic.wordpress.com |date=July 15, 2010 |access-date=March 27, 2016}}</ref> By the time he graduated high school, he was writing and playing songs that incorporated [[folk music|folk]], [[blues]], country, and gospel influences he absorbed while travelling throughout the South. In the late 1960s, he worked with [[Van Dyke Parks]] and was member of the psychedelic country band Stone Country.<ref name="LarkinCountry"/>

Young wrote many songs, including "[[Lonesome, On'ry and Mean]]" (covered by [[Waylon Jennings]]) and "Montgomery In the Rain" (covered by [[Hank Williams, Jr.]]).<ref name="LarkinCountry"/>

During the late 1970s, Young became a Buddhist and a vegetarian.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://americansongwriter.com/behind-the-song-seven-bridges-road-written-by-steve-young/david-freeland/|title=Behind the Song: The Eagles "Seven Bridges Road" « American Songwriter|date=December 13, 2019|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20191213210243/https://americansongwriter.com/behind-the-song-seven-bridges-road-written-by-steve-young/david-freeland/|access-date=October 19, 2021|archive-date=December 13, 2019}}</ref>

His best-known composition is "[[Seven Bridges Road]]", which became a hit for [[Eagles (band)|Eagles]] after including a cover of it on their 1980 live album.<ref name="LarkinCountry"/>  Earlier covers of the song were done by [[Joan Baez]], [[Tracy Nelson (singer)|Tracy Nelson]] & [[Mother Earth (American band)|Mother Earth]], [[Iain Matthews]], [[Dolly Parton]], and [[Rita Coolidge]].<ref name="LarkinCountry"/>

In 1984, Young charted the single "It's Not Supposed to Be That Way" on [[RCA Records]].<ref name="LarkinCountry"/> It peaked at No.&nbsp;84 on [[Hot Country Songs]].<ref name="whitburn">{{cite book|last=Whitburn|first=Joel|title=Hot Country Songs 1944 to 2008|publisher=Record Research, Inc|year=2008|page=485|isbn=978-0-89820-177-2}}</ref> In 1989, Young did a mini-tour in the Northwest with singer-songwriter Tim Otto, performing in Portland and Seattle. In 1991, Otto took the cover shot for Steve Young's ''Solo/Live'' CD on Watermelon Records. Townes Van Zandt wrote the liner notes. The 1996 concert recording by [[Van Dyke Parks]] entitled ''[[Moonlighting: Live at the Ash Grove]]'' (released in 1998) was headlined by Young, although he did not appear on the recordings.

In 1993, Young played a show in [[Lichtenvoorde]], The Netherlands, with [[David Olney]]. This show then was recorded by SCR Productions. Olney's track were released on his ''Live in Holland'' album in 1994.  In 2019, an album of Young's tracks was released, also titled ''Live in Holland''. David Olney took care of the liner notes, and performed on a few tracks on harmonica.

Young suffered a head injury in an October 2015 fall and his health began to deteriorate. He died after a brief stay in a [[Nashville, Tennessee]] hospice, on March 17, 2016. He was 73.<ref name=TheTennessean>{{cite news|last= Thanki |first= Juli |date= March 20, 2016 |title= 'Seven Bridges Road' Songwriter Steve Young Dead at 73|url= http://www.tennessean.com/story/entertainment/music/2016/03/19/seven-bridges-road-songwriter-steve-young-dead-73/82010980/ |access-date= October 19, 2016|newspaper =[[The Tennessean]]}}</ref><ref name=theguardian>{{cite news|last= Sweeting|first= Adam |date= March 27, 2016 |title= Steve Young obituary |url=https://www.theguardian.com/music/2016/mar/27/steve-young-obituary |access-date= October 19, 2016|newspaper =[[The Guardian]]}}</ref>
