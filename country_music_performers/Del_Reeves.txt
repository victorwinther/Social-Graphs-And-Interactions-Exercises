'''Franklin Delano Reeves''' (July 14, 1932 – January 1, 2007)<ref name="NYT">{{Cite web|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2007/01/06/obituaries/06reeves.html|title=Del Reeves, Grand Ole Opry Star, 74, Dies|agency=Associated Press|date=January 6, 2007|access-date=August 9, 2021|website=[[The New York Times]]}}</ref> was an American [[country music]] singer, best known for his "girl-watching" [[novelty songs]] of the 1960s including "[[Girl on the Billboard]]" and "The Belles of Southern Bell". He is also known for his 1968 [[truck driver|trucker]]'s anthem, "Looking at the World Through a Windshield", which demonstrated he was capable of more than just novelty songs. He became one of the most successful male [[country singer]]s of the 1960s.

==Early life==
Reeves was born in [[Sparta, North Carolina]], in July 1932 , United States, the youngest of 11 children.<ref name="allmusic">{{cite web|url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/del-reeves-mn0000814974/biography|title=Del Reeves: Biography|last=Huey|first=Steve|website=[[AllMusic]]|access-date=July 3, 2021}}</ref> He was named after U.S. President [[Franklin Delano Roosevelt]].<ref name="Foxx"/> While his older brothers served in [[World War II]], Reeves learned how to play their musical instruments.<ref name="allmusic"/> By age 12, Reeves began performing on the ''Merry Go Round Show'' on local radio station [[WPAQ]].<ref name="Foxx">{{cite journal|last=Foxx|first=Virginia|url=https://www.govinfo.gov/content/pkg/CRECB-2007-pt3/pdf/CRECB-2007-pt3-Pg3080-5.pdf|title=Honoring Del Reeves|journal=Congressional Record|volume=153|issue=3|date=February 5, 2007|page=3081|access-date=July 3, 2021}}</ref><ref name="CMT">{{cite web|last=Morris|first=Edward|url=https://www.cmt.com/news//grand-ole-opry-star-del-reeves-dead-at-73/|title=Grand Ole Opry Star Del Reeves Dead at 73|publisher=Country Music Television|date=January 4, 2007|access-date=July 3, 2021|url-status=dead|archiveurl=https://web.archive.org/web/20070108182501/http://www.cmt.com:80/news/articles/1549261/01042007/reeves_del.jhtml|archivedate=January 8, 2007}}</ref>

After high school, Reeves briefly attended [[Appalachian State University|Appalachian State College (now University)]]. He later enlisted in the [[United States Air Force]], stationed at [[Travis Air Force Base]] in [[Fairfield, California]].<ref name="CMT"/>

==Music career==
===1950s===
After completing his military service, Reeves began performing regularly on [[Chester Smith]]'s radio show on [[Modesto, California|Modesto]] station [[KTRB]] around 1955.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.hillbilly-music.com/artists/story/index.php?id=12986|title=Chester Smith|website=Hillbilly-music.com|access-date=August 9, 2021}}</ref><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/chester-smith-mn0000109459/biography|title=Chester Smith &#124; Biography & History|website=[[AllMusic]]|access-date=August 9, 2021}}</ref> Reeves made his first [[rockabilly]] recordings with [[Capitol Records]] from 1957 to 1958.<ref name="CST">{{cite web|url=https://www.countrystandardtime.com/news/newsitem.asp?xid=318|title=Del Reeves dies at 73|publisher=Country Standard Time|date=January 2, 2007|access-date=July 3, 2021}}</ref><ref name="Billboard 1958"/>

In 1958, Reeves began hosting a television variety show on [[Stockton, California|Stockton]] station [[KOVR]].<ref name="Billboard 1958">{{cite journal|url=https://worldradiohistory.com/Archive-All-Music/Billboard/50s/1958/Billboard%201958-04-14.pdf|title=Folk talent & tunes|last=Sachs|first=Bill|journal=The Billboard|date=April 14, 1958|pages=14, 46|volume=70|issue=15|access-date=July 3, 2021|via=World Radio History}}</ref>

===1960s–2000s===
In 1961, Reeves signed with [[Decca Records]] and recorded the song that would become his first charting single, "Be Quiet Mind".<ref name="allmusic"/> Moving to [[Nashville]] in 1962, Reeves and wife Ellen Schiell co-wrote "Sing a Little Song of Heartache", a top five hit for [[Rose Maddox]] that year.<ref name="CMT"/><ref name="Trott"/>

After stints with [[Reprise Records]] in 1963 and [[Columbia Records]] in 1964, Reeves signed with [[United Artists Records]].<ref name="Trott">{{cite book|last=Trott|first=Walt|chapter=Del Reeves|chapter-url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tLZz02EzmBYC&pg=PA434|title=The Encyclopedia of Country Music|editor-first=Paul|editor-last=Kingsbury|place=New York|publisher=Oxford University Press|pages=434–435|isbn=978-0-19-517608-7|year=1998}}</ref> Reeves became known as "The Doodle-Oo-Doo-Doo Kid" for the sound effect he added to his songs.<ref name="Wolff">{{cite book|chapter=Del Reeves|title=Country Music: The Rough Guide|last=Wolff|first=Kurt|place=London|publisher=Rough Guides|year=2000|page=322|isbn=1-85828-534-8}}</ref>

Under United Artists, he had his first number-one hit with "The Girl on the Billboard" in 1965.<ref name="allmusic"/> His follow-up, "The Belles of the Southern Bell", made the country top five.<ref name="allmusic"/> His success continued throughout the rest of the 1960s. Songs that became hits during this time are "Be Glad" and "Good Time Charlie's".<ref name="allmusic"/> In the late 1960s, he appeared in several Hollywood films, including a starring role in ''Cottonpickin' Chickenpickers'', and a supporting role in [[Burt Reynolds]]' first big movie ''[[Sam Whiskey]]''.<ref name="CMT"/><ref name="Trott"/>

In the 1970s, he released a series of duets with [[Bobby Goldsboro]] and [[Penny DeHaven]]. He also returned to television, hosting the syndicated TV program, ''Del Reeves' Country Carnival''.<ref name="allmusic"/>

His last big hit was "The Philadelphia Fillies".  His career declined in the mid-1970s, and then started to slowly move away from country music, although he recorded some duets with [[Billie Jo Spears]] in 1976.<ref name="allmusic"/> He had hit songs on the country chart most years until 1982.

In 1979, Reeves left his musical career to pursue a career as a music executive; he would eventually help [[Billy Ray Cyrus]] score his first major record deal.<ref name="allmusic"/> He continued to record in the 1980s on a reduced scale for smaller labels.

==Personal life==
Reeves married Ellen Schiell in 1956 at the [[Stanislaus County, California|Stanislaus County]] fair in California; they had three children.<ref name="Trott"/><ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.williamsfh.com/obituaries/Ellen-Reeves-3/|title=Ellen Reeves - View Obituary & Service Information|website=Williamsfh.com|access-date=August 9, 2021}}</ref> Reeves died of [[emphysema]] on January 1, 2007, at age 74, at his home in [[Centerville, Tennessee]].<ref name="NYT"/><ref name="Foxx"/><ref name="CMT"/>
