'''Connie Britton''' (born '''Constance Elaine Womack'''; March 6, 1967)<ref>{{cite news|title=Monitor|newspaper=[[Entertainment Weekly]]|date=Mar 8, 2013|issue=1249|page=20}}</ref> is an American actress. Britton made her feature film debut in the independent comedy-drama film ''[[The Brothers McMullen]]'' (1995), and the following year, she was cast as Nikki Faber on the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]] sitcom ''[[Spin City]]''. She later starred in the short-lived sitcoms ''[[The Fighting Fitzgeralds]]'' (2001) and ''[[Lost at Home]]'' (2003), and appeared in several films, most notably the sports drama film ''[[Friday Night Lights (film)|Friday Night Lights]]'' (2004) and the thriller film ''[[The Last Winter (2006 film)|The Last Winter]]'' (2006).<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/celebrity/connie_britton|title=Connie Britton|publisher=Rotten Tomatoes|access-date=7 December 2018}}</ref>

Britton starred as [[Tami Taylor]] on the [[NBC]]/[[DirecTV]] drama series ''[[Friday Night Lights (TV series)|Friday Night Lights]]'' from 2006 to 2011. For this role, she received positive reviews from critics and was nominated for two [[Primetime Emmy Award|Emmy Award]]s for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series|Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series]].<ref name=yahoo>{{cite web |url=https://movies.yahoo.com/person/connie-britton/biography.html |title=Connie Britton Biography |publisher=[[Yahoo! Movies]] |access-date=2012-10-22}}</ref>

In 2011, she starred as [[Vivien Harmon]] in the first season of [[FX (TV channel)|FX]] horror-drama series ''[[American Horror Story]]'', for which she was nominated for the [[Primetime Emmy Award|Emmy Award]] for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie|Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie]].<ref>{{cite news |url=https://www.huffpost.com/entry/american-horror-story-emmys_n_1687288 |title='American Horror Story' Earns 17 Emmy Nominations |work=HuffPost|access-date=November 24, 2019 |first=Laura |last=Prudom |date=July 19, 2012}}</ref> She reprised her role for the eighth season of the show, ''[[American Horror Story: Apocalypse|Apocalypse]]''. Britton starred as country singer [[Rayna Jaymes]] in the ABC/[[CMT (American TV channel)|CMT]] musical drama series ''[[Nashville (2012 TV series)|Nashville]]'' from 2012 to 2018,<ref name="tvline">{{cite news|last1=Ausiello|first1=Michael|title=Connie Britton Talks 'Devastating' Nashville Twist ('There Wasn't Any Alternative'), Teases Rayna's 'Return' |url=http://tvline.com/2017/02/23/connie-britton-leaving-nashville-rayna-death-interview/ |date=February 23, 2017 |work=TVLine}}</ref> for which she was nominated for another Emmy Award for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series|Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series]] and a [[Golden Globe Award]] for [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Television Series Drama|Best Actress – Television Series Drama]].<ref name=gg>{{cite news|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/race/first-golden-globe-nominations-announced-401941|title=Golden Globe Nominations: 'Lincoln' Leads With 7, 'Game Change' Tops TV|author=<!--Not stated-->|work=The Hollywood Reporter|access-date=23 March 2015|date=December 13, 2012}}</ref>

In 2016, Britton had a recurring role as socialite [[Faye Resnick]] in the [[The People v. O.J. Simpson: American Crime Story|first season]] of [[FX (TV channel)|FX]] true crime anthology series ''[[American Crime Story]]''. In 2018, she starred as Abby Clark in the Fox procedural drama series ''[[9-1-1 (TV series)|9-1-1]]'' as a main role in the first season and a guest in the third season, and received a [[Golden Globe Award]] nomination for [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Miniseries or Television Film|Best Actress – Miniseries or Television Film]] for her performance as Debra Newell in the [[Bravo (American TV network)|Bravo]] true crime anthology series ''[[Dirty John (TV series)|Dirty John]]''.<ref name="deadline.com">{{cite web|url=https://deadline.com/2018/12/golden-globe-nominations-2019-full-list-1202515029/|title=Golden Globes Nominations: 'Vice', 'Versace' Lead Wide-Ranging Field – Complete List |first1=Patrick|last1=Hipes|date=December 6, 2018|access-date=December 7, 2018}}</ref> In 2021, Britton starred in the [[HBO]] satire comedy-drama series ''[[The White Lotus]]'' alongside [[Jennifer Coolidge]] and [[Steve Zahn]],<ref>{{Cite web|last=Radish|first=Christina|date=2021-08-01|title=Connie Britton & Steve Zahn on the Complicated Marriage at the Heart of 'The White Lotus'|url=https://collider.com/the-white-lotus-connie-britton-steve-zahn-interview-hbo-mike-white/|url-status=live|access-date=2021-12-08|website=Collider|language=en-US|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20210801173732/https://collider.com/the-white-lotus-connie-britton-steve-zahn-interview-hbo-mike-white/ |archive-date=August 1, 2021 }}</ref> for which she was nominated for an Emmy Award for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Limited or Anthology Series or Movie|Outstanding Supporting Actress in a Limited or Anthology Series or Movie]].<ref>{{Cite web |last=Emmy Awards |date=July 12, 2022 |title=Outstanding Supporting Actress In A Limited Or Anthology Series Or Movie - 2022 |url=https://www.emmys.com/awards/nominees-winners/2022/outstanding-supporting-actress-in-a-miniseries-or-a-movie |access-date=July 12, 2022 |website=[[Academy of Television Arts & Sciences|Television Academy]]}}</ref>

In feature films, Britton starred in the romantic comedy-drama ''[[Seeking a Friend for the End of the World]]'' (2012), the romantic comedy ''[[The To Do List]]'' (2013), the comedy-dramas ''[[This Is Where I Leave You]]'' (2014) and ''[[Me and Earl and the Dying Girl (film)|Me and Earl and the Dying Girl]]'' (2015), the action comedy ''[[American Ultra]]'' (2015), the period drama ''[[Professor Marston and the Wonder Women]]'' (2017), the comedy-drama ''[[The Land of Steady Habits]]'' (2018), the drama ''[[Bombshell (2019 film)|Bombshell]]'' (2019) and the thriller ''[[Promising Young Woman]]'' (2020).

==Early life and education==
Britton was born Constance Elaine Womack in [[Boston]], Massachusetts, to Linda Jane (née Cochran) and Edgar Allen Womack, Jr., who was a [[physicist]] and an energy company executive.<ref name=bio>{{cite web|url=http://www.filmreference.com/film/18/Connie-Britton.html |title=Connie Britton Biography |publisher=Film Reference |access-date=2012-10-22}}</ref><ref name="moremagazine">{{cite web |url=http://www.more.com/entertainment/celebrities-movies-tv-music/connie-britton-her-risky-path-happiness?page=2 |title=Connie Britton on Her Risky Path to Happiness |publisher=More Magazine |date=March 2013 |access-date=July 25, 2014 |first=Leah |last=Rozen |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140727160703/http://www.more.com/entertainment/celebrities-movies-tv-music/connie-britton-her-risky-path-happiness?page=2 |archive-date=July 27, 2014 }}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=https://select.nytimes.com/gst/abstract.html?res=FB0B17FA3E5B1A7B93CBAB1789D95F478685F9 |title=Womack-Cochran |date=1963-12-29 |work=[[The New York Times]] |access-date=2012-10-22}}</ref> She spent her early years in [[Rockville, Maryland]].<ref name="moremagazine"/>

When she was seven years old, she moved with her parents and her [[fraternal twin]] sister, Cynthia,<ref>{{cite web|title=Interview with Chris Hardwick|publisher=Nerdist Podcast #157|url=http://www.nerdist.com/2012/01/nerdist-podcast-157-connie-britton/|access-date=10 January 2012|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121001234632/http://www.nerdist.com/2012/01/nerdist-podcast-157-connie-britton/|archive-date=1 October 2012|url-status=dead}}</ref> to [[Lynchburg, Virginia]], where she attended [[E. C. Glass High School]] and performed in plays in the E. C. Glass High School Theater; her photo is displayed in the E. C. Glass Alumni Theater. She majored in [[Asian studies]] with a concentration in Chinese at [[Dartmouth College]], and studied at the [[Beijing Normal University]]<ref name="moremagazine"/> during her freshman summer with [[Kirsten Gillibrand]], who was later elected to the United States Senate.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://thedartmouth.com/2012/10/11/arts/britton |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121013163116/http://thedartmouth.com/2012/10/11/arts/britton |url-status=dead |archive-date=October 13, 2012 |title='Nashville' star Connie Britton '89 sits down to discuss her new show |date=October 11, 2012 |first=Sydney |last=Ayers |access-date=2012-10-13 }}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.politico.com/blogs/click/2013/10/connie-britton-on-roomie-kirsten-gillibrand-174530.html |title=Connie Britton on roomie Kirsten Gillibrand |date=October 8, 2013 |access-date=November 28, 2013 |first=McDevitt |last=Caitlin |publisher=Politico}}</ref>

After graduating in 1989, Britton (then Womack) moved to New York City, where she spent two years at the [[Neighborhood Playhouse School of the Theatre]] studying with [[Sanford Meisner]].<ref>Connie Britton on Off Camera with Sam Jones, November 12, 2015</ref>

==Career==

===1995–2005: Early work===
[[File:ConnieBritton06TIFF.jpg|thumb|upright|Britton at the [[2006 Toronto International Film Festival]]]]
While studying at the Neighborhood Playhouse, Britton (then Womack) made her New York theatrical debut in [[Caroline Kava]]'s ''The Early Girl'' at The Courtyard Playhouse.<ref name=yahoo/>  Britton played seasoned prostitute Laurel opposite Cooper Lawrence, who played Joan. Britton's performance, while well received, nearly got her ousted from the Neighborhood Playhouse program, which prohibited students from taking professional employment during their course of study. After graduating, Britton spent two more years working in [[off-Broadway]] theatre productions. In 1995, Britton made her feature film debut in [[Edward Burns]]'s comedy-drama ''[[The Brothers McMullen]]''.<ref>{{cite web|author=Peter Travers |url=https://www.rollingstone.com/movies/reviews/the-brothers-mcmullen-19950809 |title=The Brothers McMullen &#124; Movie Reviews |publisher=Rolling Stone |date=1995-08-09 |access-date=2014-06-03}}</ref> She moved to [[Los Angeles]] after the film's success.<ref name=yahoo/>

After ''The Brothers McMullen'', Britton was cast as Heather in a recurring role in the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]] sitcom ''[[Ellen (TV series)|Ellen]]'', and starred as Cammie Barbash in the unsold [[Fox Broadcasting Company|Fox]] pilot ''Pins and Needles''. Britton was one of two finalists for the part of Dorothy Boyd opposite [[Tom Cruise]] in [[Cameron Crowe]]'s romantic comedy-drama ''[[Jerry Maguire]]'' (1996), but lost the role to [[Renée Zellweger]].<ref>{{cite news|url= https://www.nydailynews.com/entertainment/tv-movies/connie-britton-heartbreak-losing-jerry-maguire-article-1.1263554|title= Connie Britton reveals she lost 'Jerry Maguire' role to Renée Zellweger: 'It was heartbreak'|date=February 13, 2013|work=[[New York Daily News]]|access-date=August 7, 2021}}</ref> In 1996, she began co-starring as Nikki Faber in the ABC sitcom ''[[Spin City]]'' opposite [[Michael J. Fox]].<ref name=yahoo/>  Her character was written out of the show when [[Charlie Sheen]] replaced Fox. She also appeared in the romantic comedy-drama ''[[No Looking Back (1998 film)|No Looking Back]]'' (1998) and the mystery comedy-drama ''[[Looking for Kitty]]'' (2004), both directed by Edward Burns. In 2001, she co-starred opposite [[Chris Eigeman]] and [[Jamie Harris (actor)|Jamie Harris]] in the romantic comedy ''[[The Next Big Thing (film)|The Next Big Thing]]''. She later co-starred in the independent films ''The Life Coach'', ''Special Ed'', ''The Lather Effect'' and ''[[The Last Winter (2006 film)|The Last Winter]]''.

After leaving ''Spin City'', Britton had a recurring role as Maggie Kimble Hume in the short-lived [[CBS]] crime drama ''[[The Fugitive (2000 TV series)|The Fugitive]]''. In 2001, she played Gertrude Temple, [[Shirley Temple]]'s bad mother in the ABC television film ''Child Star: The Shirley Temple Story'', based on Temple's 1988 autobiography.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.nytimes.com/movies/movie/246180/Child-Star-The-Shirley-Temple-Story/overview |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20140606235132/http://www.nytimes.com/movies/movie/246180/Child-Star-The-Shirley-Temple-Story/overview |url-status=dead |archive-date=2014-06-06 |department=Movies & TV Dept. |work=[[The New York Times]] |author=Mark Deming |date=2014 |title=Child-Star-The-Shirley-Temple-Story - Trailer - Cast - Showtimes |access-date=2014-06-03}}</ref> In the same year, she starred as Sophie Fitzgerald in the short-lived [[NBC]] sitcom ''[[The Fighting Fitzgeralds]]''.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://usatoday30.usatoday.com/life/columns/candy/2001-05-02-candy.htm |title= What nobody learned from 'Peaks' |work=USA Today |date=2001-05-02 |access-date=2014-06-03}}</ref> In 2001, Britton also had a recurring role in the NBC political drama ''[[The West Wing]]'' in the [[The West Wing (season 3)|third season]], appearing in the two-part premiere titled "[[Manchester (The West Wing)|Manchester]]" and the sixth episode titled "Gone Quiet" as Connie Tate, a member of Bruno Gianelli's team. In 2002, she returned to ABC and starred as Rachel Davis in the short-lived sitcom ''[[Lost at Home]]''.  In 2006, she had a recurring role in the Fox espionage thriller ''[[24 (television)|24]]'' in the [[24 (season 5)|fifth season]] as [[Diane Huxley]], a landlady and a brief girlfriend of [[Jack Bauer]] ([[Kiefer Sutherland]]).

===2006–2011: Further recognition===
[[File:Connie Britton and Kyle Chandler crop.jpg|left|thumb|Britton and Kyle Chandler in 2008]]
Britton co-starred opposite [[Kyle Chandler]] in the NBC/[[DirecTV]] drama series ''[[Friday Night Lights (TV series)|Friday Night Lights]]'' as [[Tami Taylor]], the wife of a head football coach [[Eric Taylor (Friday Night Lights)|Eric Taylor]] (Chandler), who becomes a high school guidance counselor.<ref name=NYTFeb2013>{{Cite news |title=Connie Britton Is a Late Bloomer|author=Dominus, Susan|newspaper=The New York Times|date=February 13, 2013|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2013/02/17/magazine/connie-britton-is-a-late-bloomer.html|access-date=June 3, 2013}}</ref> The show ran for five seasons from October 3, 2006, to February 9, 2011. During that time, Britton developed a devoted following for her performance – described by ''The New York Times'' as "something of an icon, a 40-something sex symbol and role model."<ref name=NYTFeb2013/> She was first cast opposite [[Billy Bob Thornton]] in the film version of the series, ''[[Friday Night Lights (film)|Friday Night Lights]]'' (2004).<ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.nypost.com/p/blogs/popwrap/connie_britton_the_audience_will_x0ZvWYxnM5Db9A5HpCdu6H |title=Connie Britton: 'The audience will feel socked in the gut'|publisher=New York Post|access-date=2012-10-22|url-status=dead|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121014175934/http://www.nypost.com/p/blogs/popwrap/connie_britton_the_audience_will_x0ZvWYxnM5Db9A5HpCdu6H|archive-date=October 14, 2012 }}</ref> For her role in the show, she was nominated for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series]] in 2010 and 2011, for [[TCA Award for Individual Achievement in Drama]] in 2007 and 2008, and was awarded [[Satellite Award for Best Actress – Television Series Drama]] in 2010.{{citation needed|date=December 2017}}

In 2009, Britton co-starred opposite [[Carla Gugino]] and her ''Friday Night Lights'' co-star [[Adrianne Palicki]] in the comedy ''[[Women in Trouble]]'', and co-starred opposite [[Jackie Earle Haley]], [[Kyle Gallner]] and [[Rooney Mara]] in the 2010 remake of ''[[A Nightmare on Elm Street (2010 film)|A Nightmare on Elm Street]]'' as Dr. Gwendoline "Gwen" Holbrook.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.bloody-disgusting.com/news/17883|title=Nancy's Mother Talks 'Elm Street' Remake and Keeping it Fresh|date=October 29, 2009 |publisher=Bloody-disgusting.com|access-date=2012-10-22}}</ref> In 2011, she co-starred opposite [[Pamela Adlon]] in the romantic comedy ''[[Conception (film)|Conception]]''.<ref>{{cite news|url= https://www.huffpost.com/entry/conception-trailer_n_1237379 |title='Conception' Trailer: Connie Britton, David Arquette And More Take On The Road To Parenthood (VIDEO) |work=HuffPost|date= January 27, 2012|access-date=2019-11-22}}</ref> She appeared on the [[WBEZ]] radio reading show ''[[This American Life]]''{{'s}} 429th episode, "Will They Know Me at Home?", in which she performed monologues from [[David Finkel]]'s nonfiction book ''[[The Good Soldiers]]''.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.thisamericanlife.org/radio-archives/episode/429/will-they-know-me-back-home |title=Will They Know Me Back Home? |date=March 11, 2011 |publisher=This American Life |access-date=2012-10-22}}</ref>

In 2011, Britton starred in the first season in the [[FX (TV channel)|FX]] horror drama ''[[American Horror Story]]''. She played [[Vivien Harmon]], who relocates with her family to California after a series of tragic marital and family issues.<ref>{{cite magazine |url=https://ew.com/article/2011/03/18/connie-britton-ryan-murphy-american-horror-stor/|title='Friday Night Lights' star Connie Britton cast in Ryan Murphy's 'American Horror Story' pilot|last=Stransky |first=Tanner|magazine=Entertainment Weekly|date=March 18, 2011|access-date=March 18, 2011}}</ref> Unfortunately for The Harmons, the new house they purchase quickly reveals itself to be haunted. For this role she was nominated for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Miniseries or a Movie]] in 2012. Britton had stated when she wrapped ''American Horror Story'' she would be taking a couple of months off to be with her new adopted son, but that she would then begin to re-focus on developing her FX drama collaboration with [[David O. Russell]], but the project went into "[[development hell]]".<ref>{{cite web |url=http://www.tvline.com/2011/02/exclusive-connie-britton-developing-fx-drama-with-the-fighters-david-o-russell/|title=Exclusive: Connie Britton Developing FX Drama with The Fighter's David O. Russell|first=Michael|last=Ausiello|date=February 4, 2011|work=TVLine}}</ref>

===2012–present: Continued success===
On March 6, 2012, Britton signed on to star in and produce the [[American Broadcasting Company|ABC]]/[[Country Music Television|CMT]] musical drama series, ''[[Nashville (2012 TV series)|Nashville]]'' created by Academy Award winner [[Callie Khouri]]. The series ran on ABC and CMT for six seasons from October 10, 2012, to July 26, 2018. She played [[Rayna Jaymes]], a 40-year-old renowned [[Country music|country]] singer whose stardom is beginning to fade.<ref>{{cite web|first=Nellie|last=Andreeva|url=https://www.deadline.com/2012/03/connie-britton-nashville-abc-pilot/|title=Connie Britton To Star In ABC's 'Nashville'|website=[[Deadline Hollywood]]|date=March 6, 2012|access-date=March 6, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite magazine|first=Nellie|last=Andreeva|url=https://ew.com/article/2012/03/06/connie-britton-nashville/|title=Connie Britton cast in ABC drama pilot 'Nashville'|magazine=[[Entertainment Weekly]]|date=March 6, 2012|access-date=March 6, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|author= Michael Ausiello|author-link= Michael Ausiello|url=http://www.tvline.com/2012/03/connie-britton-nashville-abc|title=Scoop: Connie Britton Heads for Nashville With Lead in ABC Musical Drama Pilot|work=TVLine|date=March 6, 2012|access-date=March 6, 2012}}</ref> Britton's performance received critical praise,<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/review/nashville-tv-review-hayden-panettiere-375695|title=Nashville: TV Review|first=Tim|last=Goodman|work=The Hollywood Reporter|access-date=March 23, 2015|date=October 5, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.nypost.com/p/entertainment/tv/smash_ville_sIkb3aSXFAvL9OGXlUFXmL|title='Nashville' is a smash|work=New York Post|access-date=March 23, 2015|first=Linda|last=Stasi|date=October 9, 2012}}</ref><ref>{{cite web|url=http://unclebarky.com/reviews_files/18ff7d23d2aa910d218e209140a2a1fe-1437.html|title=Singing its praises: ABC's ''Nashville'' is fall's best newcomer - Uncle Barky's Bytes|access-date=March 23, 2015}}</ref> and she was nominated for a fourth time for a [[Primetime Emmy Award]] for [[Primetime Emmy Award for Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series|Outstanding Lead Actress in a Drama Series]] and a [[Golden Globe Award]] for [[Golden Globe Award for Best Actress – Television Series Drama|Best Actress – Television Series Drama]] for the first time for this role.<ref name=gg/><ref name=NYTFeb2013/> Britton, for her part, said that the most nerve-wracking part of her role, at least in the beginning, was the singing the role requires of her. The show was cancelled in May 2016 by ABC.<ref name="Nashville Canceled">{{cite news|last=Hibberd|first=James|title=Nashville Canceled in surprise move by ABC|url=https://www.ew.com/article/2016/05/12/nashville-canceled|date=May 12, 2016}}</ref> However, in June 2016, [[Country Music Television|CMT]] picked up the series for a fifth season.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://deadline.com/2016/06/nashville-picked-up-season-5-cmt-hulu-1201769711/|title='Nashville' Picked Up For Season 5 By CMT With Next-Day Play on Hulu|website=Deadline Hollywood|first=Nellie|last=Andreeva|date=June 9, 2016|access-date=June 10, 2016}}</ref> In February 2017, Britton left ''Nashville'' during the fifth season,<ref>{{cite news|title='Nashville' Shocker Explained: Connie Britton Opens Up About Devastating Ending|url=https://www.hollywoodreporter.com/live-feed/nashville-season-5-rayna-dies-connie-britton-exit-interview-979193|last=Goldberg|first=Lesley|work=[[The Hollywood Reporter]]|date=February 23, 2017|access-date=February 24, 2017}}</ref> but returned for the series finale in July 2018 as a guest star.<ref>{{cite web|last=McHenry|first=Jackson|title=Why Connie Britton Came Back to Nashville for the Series Finale |url=https://www.vulture.com/2018/07/why-connie-britton-came-back-for-the-nashville-series-finale.html|website=Vulture|access-date=November 25, 2019|date=July 27, 2018}}</ref>

In 2012, Britton co-starred opposite Edward Burns in the comedy-drama ''[[The Fitzgerald Family Christmas]]''.<ref>{{cite news|url=https://www.huffpost.com/entry/fitzgerald-family-christmas-trailer-ed-burns_n_1968408|title='The Fitzgerald Family Christmas' Trailer: Ed Burns Returns to Long Island For The Holidays|work=HuffPost|date=2012-10-15|access-date=2019-11-24}}</ref> The film was met with positive reviews from critics.<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.rottentomatoes.com/m/the_fitzgerald_family_christmas|title=The Fitzgerald Family Christmas|publisher=Rotten Tomatoes|access-date=2014-06-03}}</ref> That same year, she had co-starred in the comedy-drama ''[[Seeking a Friend for the End of the World]]'' opposite [[Steve Carell]] and [[Keira Knightley]].<ref>{{cite web|author=A. O. Scott|url=https://www.nytimes.com/2012/06/22/movies/steve-carell-in-seeking-a-friend-for-the-end-of-the-world.html|title=Steve Carell in 'Seeking a Friend for the End of the World'|newspaper=[[The New York Times]]|date=2012-06-21|access-date=2017-05-26}}</ref>

In 2013, Britton co-starred opposite [[Aubrey Plaza]] in the comedy ''[[The To Do List]]'', where she played the mother of Plaza's character.<ref>{{cite web|last=Greco|first=Patti|url=https://www.vulture.com/2013/07/to-do-list-director-maggie-carey-interview.html|title=To-Do List Director on Casting Connie Britton|publisher=Vulture|date=2013-07-23|access-date=2014-06-03}}</ref> Britton returned to Austin, Texas, where ''Friday Night Lights'' was filmed, to co-star opposite [[Harry Connick Jr.]] and fellow actors and musicians [[Willie Nelson]], [[Lyle Lovett]] and [[Kris Kristofferson]] in the Christmas family drama ''[[Angels Sing]]''. In 2014, Britton co-starred opposite [[Adam Driver]] as a [[Cougar (slang)|cougar]] therapist in the comedy-drama ''[[This Is Where I Leave You]]'', based on [[Jonathan Tropper]]'s 2009 best-selling novel.<ref>{{cite magazine|url=https://ew.com/article/2013/04/11/connie-britton-adam-driver-this-is-where-i-leave-you/|title=Connie Britton joins Tina Fey in all-star 'This Is Where I Leave You'|magazine=[[Entertainment Weekly]]|date=April 11, 2013|access-date=2019-11-22|first=Anthony|last=Breznican}}</ref>

In 2015, she co-starred opposite [[Thomas Mann (actor)|Thomas Mann]], [[RJ Cyler]] and [[Olivia Cooke]] in the comedy-drama ''[[Me and Earl and the Dying Girl (film)|Me and Earl and the Dying Girl]]'', directed by [[Alfonso Gomez-Rejon]], and based on [[Jesse Andrews]]'‍ [[Me and Earl and the Dying Girl|2012 novel of the same name]], a wry coming-of-age story about Greg (Mann), a teenage oddball forced to befriend Rachel (Cooke), a classmate with leukemia. ==Personal life==
[[File:Connie Britton 2012.jpg|left|thumb|Britton in 2012]]
Britton uses her married name as her stage name. She met investment banker John Britton at [[Alpha Delta (Dartmouth)|Alpha Delta House]] at [[Dartmouth College]]. They moved to Manhattan together in 1989, married on October 5, 1991, and divorced in 1995.<ref>{{cite web|title=Connie Britton- Biography|url=https://movies.yahoo.com/person/connie-britton/biography.html;_ylt=AsVKjtZmiV45Q6sTbjbsk9r3TssF;_ylu=X3oDMTI2ajBnaDlrBG1pdANQZXJzb24gRW50aXR5IEFib3V0BHBvcwMxBHNlYwNNZWRpYUVudGl0eUFib3V0TGlua3NQYWNrYWdlQXNzZW1ibHk-;_ylg=X3oDMTE2Z2ppM3RwBGludGwDdXMEbGFuZwNlbi11cwRwc3RhaWQDBHBzdGNhdAMEcHQD;_ylv=3|publisher=[[Yahoo! Movies]]|access-date=June 17, 2013}}</ref>

While at Dartmouth in the late 1980s, Britton studied [[Chinese language|Chinese]] and lived for a summer in Beijing with future US Senator [[Kirsten Gillibrand]].<ref>{{cite web |url=http://thedartmouth.com/2012/10/11/arts/britton |title={{'-}}Nashville' star Connie Britton '89 sits down to discuss her new show |date=October 11, 2012 |first=Sydney |last=Ayers |access-date=October 8, 2013 |archive-date=October 13, 2012 |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20121013163116/http://thedartmouth.com/2012/10/11/arts/britton |url-status=dead }}</ref><ref>{{cite news|url=http://www.politico.com/blogs/click/2013/10/connie-britton-on-roomie-kirsten-gillibrand-174530.html |title=Connie Britton on roomie Kirsten Gillibrand |date=October 8, 2013 |access-date=November 28, 2013 |first=McDevitt |last=Caitlin |work=Politico}}</ref> In a 2012 interview on [[NPR]], Britton said of the experience: "I always wanted to be an actor. But when I went to college, I had to fulfill a language requirement and so I thought it would be really cool to do it speaking Chinese. My Chinese these days is real, real shaky. Let's put it this way: these days, my singing is better than my Chinese."<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.npr.org/2012/10/07/162382561/tvs-britton-fights-to-stay-in-nashvilles-lights |title=TV's Britton Fights To Stay In Nashville's Lights |date=7 October 2012 |publisher=NPR|access-date=7 October 2012}}</ref>
