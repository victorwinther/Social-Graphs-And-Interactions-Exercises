'''Cyrus Whitfield''' "'''Johnny'''" '''Bond''' (June 1, 1915 – June 12, 1978) was an American [[country music]] singer-songwriter, guitarist and composer and publisher, who co-founded a music publishing firm. He was active in the music industry from 1940 until the late 1970s.

==Early years==
Bond was born in [[Enville, Oklahoma]], and grew up on several small farms in Oklahoma. As a youngster, he was influenced musically by records that his parents played. He learned basics of music as a member of his high school's brass band. While in high school he bought a ukulele, but soon he switched to playing the guitar.<ref name="c">{{cite book|last1=Carlin|first1=Richard|title=Country|date=2005|publisher=Infobase Publishing|isbn=9780816069774|page=19|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=M8p_WFCDe7AC&q=%22Cyrus+Whitfield+Bond%22&pg=PA19|access-date=December 24, 2017|language=en}}</ref>

== Performing ==
Bond first performed on radio in [[Oklahoma City]] when he was 19 years old. In 1937, he began performing with [[Jimmy Wakely]] and Scotty Harrell in the Bell Boys trio, named after the Bell Clothing Company, which sponsored the group<ref name="ecm">{{cite book|title=The Encyclopedia of Country Music|date=2012|publisher=Oxford University Press|isbn=9780199920839|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=tLZz02EzmBYC&q=%22Cyrus+Whitfield+Bond%22&pg=PT131|access-date=December 24, 2017|language=en}}</ref> on radio station [[WKY]] in Oklahoma City, Oklahoma. He went on to join ''[[Gene Autry's Melody Ranch]]'' in 1940.<ref name="amgc">{{cite book|last1=Erlewine|first1=Michael|title=All Music Guide to Country: The Experts' Guide to the Best Recordings in Country Music|date=1997|publisher=Hal Leonard Corporation|isbn=9780879304751|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=7Mo7xm-X1r4C&q=%22Cyrus+Whitfield+Bond%22&pg=PA44|access-date=December 24, 2017|language=en}}</ref>

He also performed with his own group, the Red River Valley Boys.

''The Encyclopedia of Country Music'' says that the Bond-Wakely-Harrell trio "pulled a clever musical scam" by recording for two companies under different names: the Jimmy Wakely Trio (for [[Decca Records]]) and Johnny Bond & the Cimarron Boys (for [[Columbia Records]]).<ref name="ecm" />

Bond also acted in more than 40 films,<ref name="amgc" /> beginning with ''[[Saga of Death Valley]]'' (1939)<ref name="ro">{{cite book|last1=Talevski|first1=Nick|title=Rock Obituaries – Knocking On Heaven's Door|date=2010|publisher=Omnibus Press|isbn=9780857121172|page=44|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=DykffzkFALoC&q=%22Cyrus+Whitfield+Bond%22&pg=PA44|access-date=December 24, 2017|language=en}}</ref> and including ''[[Wilson (1944 film)|Wilson]]'' and ''[[Duel in the Sun (film)|Duel in the Sun]]''.<ref>{{cite news|title=Gene Autry Comic At Buck Lake Ranch|url=https://www.newspapers.com/clip/15964570/johnny_bond/|work=Angola Herald|date=September 16, 1949|location=Indiana, Angola|page=13|via = [[Newspapers.com]]|access-date = December 23, 2017}} {{Open access}}</ref>

Beginning in 1953, Bond and [[Tex Ritter]] were hosts of the syndicated country music television series ''[[Town Hall Party]],'' which lasted seven years.<ref name="c" />

== Recording ==
Bond's first solo recordings came with [[Columbia Records]] in 1937.<ref name="c" /> He is best known for his 1947 [[chart-topper|hit]] "[[Divorce Me C.O.D.]]", one of his seven [[Top 40|top ten]] hits on the ''[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]'' country [[record chart|charts]]. In 1965 at age 50 he scored the biggest hit of his career with the comic "Ten Little Bottles", which spent four weeks at No. 2. Bond's other hits include "[[So Round, So Firm, So Fully Packed]]" (1947), "Oklahoma Waltz" (1948), "Love Song in 32 Bars" (1950), "Sick Sober and Sorry" (1951), and a cover of Charlie Ryan's "[[Hot Rod Lincoln]]" (1960).

== Composing and publishing ==
The hundreds of songs that Bond wrote include "Cimarron" and "Ten Little Bottles". He and Ritter formed Vidor Publications, a music publishing firm. He retired from performing in the 1970s to devote more time to publishing music.<ref name="c" />

== Death ==
Bond died of a stroke in 1978, at the age of 63.

== Recognition ==
Bond was elected to the [[Country Music Hall of Fame]] in 1999,<ref name="cmhf">{{cite web|title=Johnny Bond|url=https://countrymusichalloffame.org/Inductees/InducteeDetail/johnny-bond|website=Country Music Hall of Fame|access-date=December 24, 2017|archive-url=https://archive.today/20171224030747/https://countrymusichalloffame.org/Inductees/InducteeDetail/johnny-bond|archive-date=December 24, 2017|url-status=live}}</ref> and to the [[Nashville Songwriters Hall of Fame]].

== In popular culture ==
Bond's song "Stars of the Midnight Range" was featured in the role-playing video game, ''[[Fallout: New Vegas]]''. Another song of his; "Headin' Down the Wrong Highway" was on the radio in the game [[Fallout 76]].
