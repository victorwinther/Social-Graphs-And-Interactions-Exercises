'''Robert Earl Keen''' (born January 11, 1956)<ref>{{Cite web |last=Rose |first=Mike|date=January 11, 2023 |title=Today's famous birthdays list for January 11, 2023 includes celebrities Mary J. Blige, Amanda Peet |url=https://www.cleveland.com/entertainment/2023/01/todays-famous-birthdays-list-for-january-11-2023-includes-celebrities-mary-j-blige-amanda-peet.html |access-date=January 10, 2023 |website=[[Cleveland.com]]}}</ref> is an American [[Country music|country]] singer-songwriter and entertainer. Debuting with 1984's ''No Kinda Dancer'', the Houston native has recorded 20 full-length albums for independent and major record labels.  His songs been [[Cover version|covered]] by artists including [[George Strait]], [[Joe Ely]], [[Lyle Lovett]], [[The Highwaymen (country supergroup)|The Highwaymen]] and [[Nanci Griffith]].<ref name=":2">{{Cite web |date=June 29, 2022 |title=Lyle Lovett – Anthology Volume One Cowboy Man |url=https://www.discogs.com/release/8995236-Lyle-Lovett-Anthology-Volume-One-Cowboy-Man |access-date=June 29, 2022 |website=Discogs – Music Database}}</ref><ref name=":3">{{Cite web |date=June 29, 2022 |title=George Strait – One Step At A Time |url=https://www.discogs.com/release/3611380-George-Strait-One-Step-At-A-Time |access-date=June 29, 2022 |website=Discogs – Music Database}}</ref><ref name=":4">{{Cite web |date=June 29, 2022 |title=Joe Ely – Love And Danger |url=https://www.discogs.com/release/9026871-Joe-Ely-Love-And-Danger |access-date=June 29, 2022 |website=Discogs – Music Database}}</ref><ref name=":5">{{Cite web |date=June 29, 2022 |title=Highwaymen – The Road Goes On Forever |url=https://www.discogs.com/release/4378780-Highwaymen-The-Road-Goes-On-Forever |access-date=June 29, 2022 |website=Discogs – Music Database}}</ref><ref name=":6">{{Cite web |date=June 29, 2022 |title=Nanci Griffith – Lone Star State of Mind |url=https://www.discogs.com/release/1882329-Nanci-Griffith-Lone-Star-State-Of-Mind |access-date=June 29, 2022 |website=Discogs – Music Database}}</ref> Keen has toured in the U.S. and abroad.

== Early life and education ==
Keen was born and grew up in [[Houston]], [[Texas]], United States.<ref name="LarkinCountry">{{cite book|title=[[Encyclopedia of Popular Music|The Guinness Who's Who of Country Music]]|editor=[[Colin Larkin (writer)|Colin Larkin]]|publisher=[[Guinness Publishing]]|date=1993|edition=First|isbn=0-85112-726-6|page=214}}</ref> As a teenager, he was an avid reader who excelled in writing and literature classes. Keen was a fan of the British rock band [[Cream (band)|Cream]], and was influenced by country music by artists [[Willie Nelson]], [[Norman Blake (American musician)|Norman Blake]], [[Jesse Winchester]], [[Foggy Mountain Boys|Flatt and Scruggs]], [[Bill Withers]], [[Gary Stewart (singer)|Gary Stewart]], and [[Jimmie Rodgers (country singer)|Jimmie Rodgers]].<ref name="skanse">{{Cite news|last=Skanse|first=Richard|title=Robert Earl Keen: A Man Apart|newspaper=Lone Star Music Magazine|access-date=January 4, 2013|url=http://www.lonestarmusic.com/magazine/mag_html/septoct11/coverstore.html}}</ref>

After graduation from Sharpstown High School, Keen started playing guitar himself shortly thereafter, learning to play classic country covers out of a songbook the summer before starting college at [[Texas A&M University]] in [[College Station, Texas]].<ref name="LarkinCountry" /> He graduated with a Bachelor of Arts in English in 1978 and began writing songs and playing [[bluegrass music|bluegrass]] and folk music with friends, including his childhood friend (and future longtime [[fiddle]] player in his band) Bryan Duckworth. During his college years Keen lived with future musician [[Lyle Lovett]].<ref name="LarkinCountry" />

== Career ==
[[File:Robert Earl Keen (2).jpg|thumb|Keen performing in 2007]]
In 1978, Keen graduated from Texas A&M and moved to [[Austin, Texas]].<ref name="LarkinCountry" /> He performed in nightclubs and live music venues in Austin, including [[Cactus Cafe]], Emma Joe's, Alamo Lounge and [[Liberty Lunch]], [[Anderson Fair Retail Restaurant|Anderson Fair]] in Houston, and [[Gruene Hall]] in [[New Braunfels]].<ref>{{Cite web |last=Hodson |first=Christy |date=July 8, 2014 |title=Cactus Cafe & Bar |url=https://www.tshaonline.org/handbook/entries/cactus-cafe-bar |access-date=May 30, 2022 |website=Handbook of Texas Online |publisher=Texas State Historical association}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=Weber |first=Andrew |date=July 17, 2020 |title=PHOTOS: Here's A Look Back At Austin Through The Lens Of Photographer Lisa Davis |work=KUT |url=https://www.kut.org/austin/2020-07-17/photos-heres-a-look-back-at-austin-through-the-lens-of-photographer-lisa-davis |access-date=May 30, 2022}}</ref><ref>{{Cite magazine |last=Freeman |first=Jon |date=June 4, 2020 |title=Robert Earl Keen to Play Social-Distanced Shows at Texas Club |magazine=Rolling Stone |url=https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-country/robert-earl-keen-gruene-hall-coronavirus-1010093/ |access-date=May 30, 2020}}</ref> In 1983, Keen won the New Folk competition at the Kerrville Folk Festival in [[Kerrville, Texas]].<ref name="davis">{{Cite news|last=Davis|first=John T.|title=Robert Earl Keen: Can you patch together a feeling that's going to stick with somebody ten years from now?|newspaper=No Depression|url=http://archives.nodepression.com/2005/05/can-you-patch-together-a-feeling-thats-going-to-stick-with-somebody-ten-years-from-now|access-date=January 10, 2017|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20141122100717/http://archives.nodepression.com/2005/05/can-you-patch-together-a-feeling-thats-going-to-stick-with-somebody-ten-years-from-now/|archive-date=November 22, 2014|url-status=dead}}</ref><ref name=":0">{{Cite news |last=Erwin |first=Hank |date=May 20, 2022 |title=A Culture 50 Years in the Making: The Kerrville Folk Festival Experience |work=The Austin Chronicle |url=https://www.austinchronicle.com/music/2022-05-20/a-culture-50-years-in-the-making-the-kerrville-folk-festival-experience/ |access-date=May 30, 2022}}</ref> That same year, he began making his self-produced first album, ''[[No Kinda Dancer]]''.<ref name="LarkinCountry" />

Following the release of this album in 1985, Keen moved to Nashville with his future wife, Kathleen Gray. He signed a publishing deal, a new independent label deal, and signed with a national booking agent.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Sale |first=Anna |date=May 20, 2015 |title=Robert Earl Keen Quit Nashville and Stayed Married |work=Death, Sex & Money |url=https://www.wnycstudios.org/podcasts/deathsexmoney/episodes/death-sex-money-robert-earl-keen |access-date=May 30, 2022}}</ref> While in Nashville, Keen and Gray worked at [[Hatch Show Print]] shop.<ref>{{Cite book |last=Sherraden |first=Jim |title=Hatch Show Print: The History of a Great American Poster Shop |publisher=Country Music Foundation |year=2001 |isbn=978-0-8118-2856-7 |pages=109}}</ref>

Keen returned to Texas in 1987 and released his second album, ''[[The Live Album]]'', in 1988, followed by his third album, ''[[West Textures]]'' in 1989.<ref>{{Cite news |last=Sale |first=Anna |date=May 20, 2015 |title=Robert Earl Keen Quit Nashville and Stayed Married |work=Death, Sex & Money |url=https://www.wnycstudios.org/podcasts/deathsexmoney/episodes/death-sex-money-robert-earl-keen |access-date=May 30, 2022}}</ref> ''West Textures'' featured the first recording of Keen's signature song, "The Road Goes on Forever."<ref name="LarkinCountry" /> Fellow Texan [[Joe Ely]] recorded the song on his 1993 album ''Love and Danger'', along with another Keen song, "Whenever Kindness Fails".<ref name="mcleese">{{Cite news |last=McLeese |first=Don |title=Straight outta Bandera: For Robert Earl Keen, music and business coexist at home, deep in the heart of Texas |newspaper=No Depression |access-date=January 5, 2013 |url=http://archives.nodepression.com/1998/11/straight-outta-bandera/ |url-status=dead |archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20130719130938/http://archives.nodepression.com/1998/11/straight-outta-bandera/ |archive-date=July 19, 2013 }}</ref> Keen's own version of "Whenever Kindness Fails" appeared on his fourth album, 1993's ''A Bigger Piece of Sky.'' In 1994, he released ''[[Gringo Honeymoon]]'' followed by ''[[No. 2 Live Dinner]] in 1996''.<ref name="mcleese" />

Keen has continued to write and record music, while also maintaining a prodigious tour schedule.<ref name=":8">{{Cite news |date=June 27, 2022 |year=2022 |title=Live 75 |pages=8–9 |work=Pollstar}}</ref> His 1997 album, ''[[Picnic (album)|Picnic]]'', marked the beginning of his on-again, off-again relationship with major labels (both that album and 1998's ''[[Walking Distance (album)|Walking Distance]]'' were issued on Arista Records, and 2001's ''[[Gravitational Forces]]'', 2009's ''[[The Rose Hotel]]'' and 2011's ''Ready for Confetti'' were released on Lost Highway Records). Keen's other albums include 2003's ''[[Farm Fresh Onions]]'' (Audium/Koch Records) and 2005's ''[[What I Really Mean]]'' and 2006's ''[[Live at the Ryman]]'' (both on E1 Music). The producers with whom he has worked on those albums have included [[John Keane (record producer)|John Keane]], [[Gurf Morlix]], Gary Velletri, and [[Lloyd Maines]]. In 2022, his concert tour was listed as one of the most successful in the world.<ref name=":8" />

His band includes:
*Bill Whitbeck — bass, upright bass, vocals
*Tom Van Schaik — drums, vocals
*Brian Beken — fiddle, acoustic guitar, electric guitar

In August of 2011, Keen was contacted by a member of U.S. Navy about a fan of his, Petty Officer First Class Jason Workman of Seal team 6, who had been killed on August 6, 2011, with 29 other Brave Americans in Wardak Province, Tanji Valley, Afghanistan. Keen went to the memorial service for POFC. Workman and played his signature song I’m coming home. Keen covered all of his own expenses for travel and only wanted to show a token of his gratitude to a fan who made the ultimate sacrifice for his country. {{cn|date=December 2023}}

In January 2022, Keen announced that he would stop touring and performing publicly after September 2022.<ref>{{cite web |title="Time Flies" – A message from Robert Earl Keen |first=Robert Earl |last= Keen |date=January 14, 2022 |website=robertearlkeen.com |url=https://www.robertearlkeen.com/news/time-flies |access-date=March 26, 2022}}</ref> His final tour was named the "I'm Coming Home Farewell Tour" and the final leg was played September 1, 3 and 4 at [[John T. Floore Country Store|Floore's Country Store]] in [[Helotes, Texas]]. Approximately 3,000 people attended the last show on September 4 for nearly two-and-a-half hours.<ref>{{cite magazine |title="Robert Earl Keen has played his final show. He's not kidding. |first=Josh |last= Crutchmer |date=September 5, 2022 |magazine=Rolling Stone |url=https://www.rollingstone.com/music/music-country/robert-earl-keen-retires-final-concert-1234586837/ |access-date=September 7, 2022}}</ref> Despite the claims of playing his final shows and farewell tour, Keen proceeded to do several shows in 2023<ref>{{cite web |title=Robert Earl Keen Isn't Un-Retiring, But He Is Re-Appearing |date=July 17, 2023 |website=savingcountrymusic.com |url=https://www.savingcountrymusic.com/robert-earl-keen-isnt-un-retiring-but-he-is-re-appearing/ |access-date=May 7, 2024}}</ref> and lists a 2024 tour on his website<ref>{{cite web |title="Robert Earl Keen" |first=Robert Earl |last= Keen |website=robertearlkeen.com |url=https://www.robertearlkeen.com/tour |access-date=May 7, 2024}}</ref> with a dozen stops. Instagram posts on Keen's account containing photos from his "I'm Coming Home Farewell Tour" were later edited to remove reference to the "farewell" part of the tour.<ref>{{cite web |title="Robert Earl Keen" |first=Robert Earl |last= Keen |website=instagram.com |url=https://www.instagram.com/p/Ch-G10grelV/ |access-date=May 7, 2024}}</ref>
