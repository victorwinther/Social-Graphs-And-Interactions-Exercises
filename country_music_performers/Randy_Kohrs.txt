'''Randy Alan Kohrs'''<ref>{{cite web|url=https://www.ascap.com/repertory#/ace/search/workID/901216071 |title=DON'T LET YOUR WOUNDED HEART COME BETWEEN US |website=ASCAP |publisher=American Society of Composers, Authors and Publishers |access-date=April 26, 2023}}</ref> is an American multi-instrumentalist best known for his [[resonator guitar]] prowess, but he plays 13 instruments. He is also a Grammy-winning producer and recording engineer.<ref name=RK-gplayer-2011>{{cite web |url=http://www.guitarplayer.com/lessons/1014/randy-kohrs-talks-resonator-guitars-at-the-country-music-hall-of-fame/14241|title=Randy Kohrs Talks Resonator Guitars at the Country Music Hall of Fame|work=Guitar Player|accessdate=September 9, 2017|date=October 26, 2011}}</ref>

==Biography==
===Early years===
Kohrs was raised on a farm near the rural town of New Virginia, Iowa. He learned acoustic guitar from his uncle at age 8, quickly followed by resonator guitar. In his teens, Kohrs played with the Missouri bluegrass band Possum Trot. He played with them for 10 years, while also fronting a local country band. He continued to learn other instruments, including electric guitar, drums, mandolin, banjo, pedal steel, and bass.<ref name=RK-roots-2014>{{cite web |url=http://www.kkfi.org/program-episodes/taylor-brashears-jon-weisberger-more/|title=Taylor Brashears, Jon Weisberger & more|work=Music City Roots|accessdate=September 8, 2017|date=April 5, 2014}}</ref>

===Early career===
In 1995, Kohrs moved to Nashville, where [[Hank Williams III]] hired him to play in his band, then he toured in support of [[Tom T. Hall]].<ref name=RK-roots-2014 /><ref name=RK-bgtoday-2015>{{cite news|last1=Morris|first1=David|title=Remembering Miss Dixie|url=https://bluegrasstoday.com/?p=61723|accessdate=September 9, 2017|work=Bluegrass Today|date=January 17, 2015}}</ref><ref name=RK-swamp-2017>{{cite web |url=http://swampland.com/reviews/view/title:tom_t_hall_sings_miss_dixie_tom_t|title=Tom T. Hall Sings Miss Dixie & Tom T.|work=Swampland|accessdate=September 9, 2017|author=James Calemine}}</ref>

In 1998, he joined [[David Parmley]], [[Scott Vestal]] and Continental Divide, singing tenor playing dobro. He contributed his skills to the album ''Feel Good Day''.<ref name=RK-nwbn-1998>{{cite web |url=http://www.g4uxd.talktalk.net/nwbn/nl-98-07/cdrevs.htm|title=Continental Divide - Feel Good Day - Pinecastle 1073|work=North West Bluegrass News|accessdate=September 9, 2017|author=John Lawless|date=July 1, 1998}}</ref>

Then Kohrs toured with [[Holly Dunn]] for two years, and joined the [[John Cowan]] Band in 2000, playing on the album ''Always Take Me Back''.<ref name=RK-amplifier-2017>{{cite web |url=http://bgamplifier.com/music/john-cowan/article_4ceda39c-df84-52e8-a6c6-e89faa21892d.html|title=John Cowan
|work=The Ampflier|accessdate=September 9, 2017}}</ref>

Kohrs backed [[Patty Loveless]] on a taping of PBS' [[Austin City Limits]] television show. In 2003, Kohrs toured with [[Dolly Parton]] in her band the Blueniques, playing on three of her albums and performing as her opening act.<ref name=RK-dolly-2011>{{cite book 
|last=Cardwell|first=Nancy|date=July 22, 2011|title=The Words and Music of Dolly Parton: Getting to Know Country's "Iron Butterfly"|publisher=Abc-Clio |isbn=9780313378041|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=DVqsWsJyAWIC&q=randy+kohrs+dolly+parton|access-date=September 9, 2017}}</ref><ref name=RK-kalamazoo-2009>{{cite web |url=http://www.mlive.com/entertainment/kalamazoo/index.ssf/2009/01/randy_kohrs_juggles_musical_ge.html|title=Randy Kohrs will juggle musical genres at Cooper's Glen Music Festival|work=Kalamazoo Gazette|accessdate=September 9, 2017|author=Mark Wedel |date=January 7, 2009}}</ref>

===Solo career===
In 2001, Kohrs released his first solo album ''A Crack in My Armour'' on Junction Records, and formed his own band The Lites. Guests on the album included [[Scott Vestal]], [[Rickie Simpkins]], and Stuart Duncan.

Kohrs followed up in 2003 with the album ''Now It’s Empty'' on his own Left Of Center label. The album featured [[John Hughey]] on pedal steel and James Mitchell on electric guitar.<ref name=RK-roots-2014 />

Kohrs' next album was ''I’m Torn'' in 2004, which featured a duet with Dolly Parton on "It Looked Good On Paper."<ref name=RK-yuptab-2009>{{cite web |url=https://yuptab.com/im-torn-by-randy-kohrs/|title=Randy Kohrs - I'm Torn|work=Yup Tab|accessdate=September 9, 2017|date=May 14, 2009}}</ref><ref name=RK-truewest-2005>{{cite web |url=https://truewestmagazine.com/im-torn/|title=I'm Torn: Randy Kohrs|work=True West|accessdate=September 9, 2017|author=Bill Groll|date=April 1, 2005}}</ref>

In 2007, Kohrs released the ''Old Photograph'' album, which featured [[Scott Vestal]] and Scott Haas on banjo, [[Jim Hurst]], Clay Hess and Andrew Crawford on guitar, Tim Crouch and Ashley Brown on fiddle, Aaron Ramsey and Jesse Cobb on mandolin, and Jim Weaver and Darren Vincent on bass.

In 2009, Kohrs released the album ''Quicksand'' on [[Rural Rhythm Records]] with Aaron Ramsey and [[Adam Steffey]] on mandolin. Kohrs composed five of the songs on the album.<ref name=RK-std-2010>{{cite web |url=http://countrystandardtime.com/d/cdreview.asp?xid=4342|title=Randy Kohrs: Quicksand|work=Country Standard Time|accessdate=September 8, 2017|author=John Walker}}</ref>

===Other projects===
Kohrs is a recording engineer and producer, and operates Slack Key studios in Nashville, where he has recorded [[Larry Cordle]] & Lonesome Standard Time, [[Hank Williams III]], [[Jim Lauderdale]], and others.<ref name=RK-mix-2007>{{cite news|last1=Schultz|first1=Barbara|title=Randy Kohrs' Slack-Key Studio: Tearing it Up on Both Sides of the Glass|url=http://www.mixonline.com/news/profiles/randy-kohrs-slack-key-studio/365852|accessdate=September 9, 2017|work=Mix Online|date=July 1, 2007}}</ref>

In 2009, Jimmy Ross introduced the concept of a compilation album dedicated to luthier Tim Scheerhorn.<ref name=RK-unlimited-2009>{{cite web |url=http://bluegrassmusic.com/content/reviews/reviews-october-2009/|title=Various Artists: Hornography|work=Bluegrass Unlimited|accessdate=September 10, 2017|date=October 1, 2009}}</ref> Besides producing, engineering and mixing the album ''Hornography'', he released it on his Left of Center label. The album featured Kohrs, Ross, Scheerhorn, [[Rob Ickes]], and resonator guitar artists.<ref name=RK-bgtoday-2008>{{cite news|last1=Brance|title=Hornography|url=https://bluegrasstoday.com/?p=4917|accessdate=September 1, 2017|work=Bluegrass Today|date=November 5, 2008}}</ref>

===Awards===
In 2007, Kohrs won a [[Grammy award]] for his contributions to [[Jim Lauderdale]]'s album ''[[The Bluegrass Diaries]]''.<ref name=RK-academy-2007>{{cite web |url=https://www.grammy.com/grammys/artists/randy-kohrs|title=GRAMMY Award Results for Randy Kohrs|work=The Recording Academy|accessdate=September 8, 2017}}</ref>

Kohrs being nominated numerous times for Dobro Player of the Year by the International Bluegrass Music Association.

===Personal life===
Kohrs proposed to his wife Shaunna Larkin onstage during a Dolly Parton concert.<ref name=RK-cmt-2002>{{cite web |url=http://www.cmt.com/news/1456196/dolly-parton-live-here-she-comes-again/|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20200721152102/http://www.cmt.com/news/1456196/dolly-parton-live-here-she-comes-again/|url-status=dead|archive-date=July 21, 2020|title=Dolly Parton, Live: Here She Comes Again|work=CMT|accessdate=September 9, 2017|author=Craig Shelburne|date=July 22, 2002}}</ref><ref name=RK-augusta-2003>{{cite web |url=http://chronicle.augusta.com/stories/2003/08/22/rho_385689.shtml#.WbTRQnNlDgA|title=Larkin girls leave the family circle|work=The Augusta Chronicle|accessdate=September 1, 2017|date=August 22, 2003}}</ref> They got married in a ceremony at the Ryman Auditorium in Nashville in September 2003.<ref>[http://www.dollymania.net/archive092003.html Congratulations, Shaunna and Randy!], dollymania.net</ref>

Randy Kohrs married fiddler and vocalist Ashley Brown in April 2011 and divorced in 2017.<ref>[https://bluegrasstoday.com/randy-kohrs-and-the-blind-boys-of-alabama/ Randy Kohrs and the Blind Boys of Alabama], bluegrasstoday.com, May 17, 2011</ref>
