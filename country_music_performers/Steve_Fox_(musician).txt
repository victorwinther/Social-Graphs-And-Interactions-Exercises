'''Steve Fox''' is a Canadian [[country music]] singer-songwriter based in [[Nashville, Tennessee|Nashville]].<ref>{{Cite news |last=Leblanc |first=Larry |date=13 April 2002 |title=Canadian songwriters score internationally |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/227150690/fulltext/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/5 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Billboard |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref><ref name=":1">{{Cite news |last=Devitt |first=Ron |date=19 October 2002 |title=Coquitlam's Steve Fox making a name in Nashville |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/358567635/fulltext/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/1 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Coquitlam Now |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref> He is best known for writing [[Montgomery Gentry]]'s hit song "Daddy Won't Sell the Farm", which went Platinum and Gold in the United States<ref name=":1" /> and "Moving to a Small Town".<ref name=":0" /> As an independent performing artist in [[Canada]], he has toured with [[Kenny Rogers]], opened for country stars including [[Dwight Yoakam|Dwight Yoakum]] and [[Michelle Wright]], and headlined at festivals including the [[Calgary Stampede]].<ref name=":0" /><ref name=":1" /> He is credited as a songwriter on many albums<ref name=":0">{{Cite news |last=Bainas |first=Lexi |date=20 October 2002 |title=The Songwriters: Three talents present their music and the stories behind their music |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/358206061/23DA0491A6834E30PQ/4 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Cowichan Valley Citizen |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref><ref name=":1" /> and has worked with producer and publisher Joe Scaife, as well as Cal IV Entertainment.<ref>{{Cite news |date=2 October 2002 |title='Songwriters' to perform at the Tidemark |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/358373373/fulltext/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/6 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Courier-Islander |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref><ref>{{Cite news |last=Stark |first=Phyllis |date=August 18, 2001 |title=Nashville scene |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/227143831/fulltext/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/12 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Billboard |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref>

In 2001, Fox won the [[Canadian Country Music Association]]'s Songwriter of the Year Award.<ref name=":3">{{Cite news |last=North |first=Peter |date=24 January 2001 |title=With a little help from his friends ...: Gord Bamford releases new album Thursday night |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/252842435/fulltext/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/40 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Edmonton Journal |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref> He was nominated for CD of the Year for his album ''Small World''<ref name=":1" /> and nominated for a [[Juno Awards|Juno Award]] for Best Male Vocalist for his 1993 album, ''The Days of My Youth'' ([[True North Records|True North]]/Sony).<ref name=":1" />

== Early life and education ==
Fox grew up in a musical family in the [[Vancouver]] suburb of [[Coquitlam|Coquitlam, British Columbia]].<ref name=":2">{{Cite news |last=Currie |first=Harry |date=23 February 2001 |title=Writing his ticket; Steve Fox's songs have made it big, now it's his turn |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/266924011/624FD8F5B114459DPQ/2 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=The Record |location=Kitchener, Ontario |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref><ref name=":1" /><ref name=":4">{{Cite news |last=Srivener |first=Leslie |date=7 January 1990 |title=Just when you think you've got the Fox boys sorted out, they pop up in another guise. They're foxy that way - but always entertaining Crazy like a fox |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/436126965/955724D0C9144110PQ/3 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=Toronto Star |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref> His father John worked in radio broadcasting, while his mother Ella was an oboe player in a chamber orchestra.<ref name=":4" /> His three brothers later pursued careers in music and acting.<ref name=":4" /> He left Coquitlam after graduating from Centennial Secondary, and traveled through Europe and the Middle East.<ref name=":1" />
== Career ==
Fox moved to [[Toronto]] in the mid-1980s to pursue a career in music, performing and writing songs for several folk, [[Rhythm and blues|R&B]], and [[rockabilly]] bands, and working as a [[Audio engineer|sound engineer]].<ref name=":2" /><ref name=":1" /> While fronting a rockabilly band called The Tin Eddies, he got his break with a solo rock LP, ''Where The Blue Moon Rises.''<ref name=":4" /><ref>{{Cite news |last=Erskine |first=Evelyn |date=27 April 1990 |title=Vega still special in mystical way |url=https://www.proquest.com/docview/239487593/fulltext/955724D0C9144110PQ/2 |access-date=2024-06-10 |work=[[The Ottawa Citizen]] |via=[[ProQuest]]}}</ref> In 1993, he had his first minor hit, ''The Days of My Youth'', on the [[True North Records|True North]]/Sony label, and was nominated for a [[Juno Awards|Juno Award]] for Best Male Vocalist.<ref name=":1" /> In 2001 he released ''Small World'' which went platinum and led to the notable singles "Small Town", "Cheap Red Wine" and "Couple On The Cake", a duet with [[Beverley Mahood]], and a video featuring [[Leslie Nielsen]]. He released the album ''Lunch With Chet'' with the single "Dream On".{{Citation needed|date=June 2024}} He is noted for writing the [[Montgomery Gentry]] single "Daddy Won't Sell The Farm", which reached No. 13 on the [[Billboard charts|Billboard music chart]] and  No. 1 on CMT; it won the Canadian Country Music Association SOCAN song of the year award.<ref name=":2" /> He wrote "24 X 24", recorded by [[Gord Bamford]].<ref name=":3" />

Fox produced records for other artists, and released his own single, "If My Life Was a Movie" in January 2007.{{Citation needed|date=June 2024}}
