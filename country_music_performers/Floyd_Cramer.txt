'''Floyd Cramer''' (October 27, 1933 – December 31, 1997) was an American [[pianist]] who became famous for his use of melodic "whole-step" attacks. He was inducted into both the [[Country Music Hall of Fame]]<ref name="bmicmhof">{{cite web |title=Country Music Hall of Fame To Welcome Floyd Cramer and Carl Smith |url=https://www.bmi.com/news/entry/20030813_Country_Music_Hall_of_Fame_To_Welcome_Floyd_Cramer_and_Carl_Smi |website=bmi.com |publisher=Broadcast Music |access-date=March 3, 2021 |date=August 13, 2003}}</ref> and the [[Rock and Roll Hall of Fame]]. His signature playing style was a cornerstone of the pop-oriented "Nashville sound" of the 1950s and 1960s.<ref name="ankenyallm">{{cite web |last1=Ankeny |first1=Jason |title=Floyd Cramer |url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/floyd-cramer-mn0000801762/biography |website=allmusic.com |publisher=AllMusic/Netaktion |access-date=March 3, 2021}}</ref> Cramer's "slip-note" or "bent-note" style, in which a [[Nonchord tone|passing note]] slides almost instantly into or away from a chordal note, influenced a generation of pianists.<ref name="nytimes98">{{cite web |title=Floyd Cramer, 64, Pianist With a Nashville Sound |url=https://www.nytimes.com/1998/01/02/arts/floyd-cramer-64-pianist-with-a-nashville-sound.html |website=[[The New York Times]] |access-date=March 2, 2021 |page=D–7 |date=January 2, 1998}}</ref> His sound became popular to the degree that he stepped out of his role as a [[sideman]] and began touring as a solo act.<ref name="fulkspresspro">{{cite web |last1=Fulks |first1=Sonny |title=Remembering Floyd Cramer . . . and one last date! |url=https://pressprosmagazine.com/remembering-floyd-cramer-and-one-last-date/ |website=pressprosmagazine.com |publisher=PressPros Magazine |access-date=March 13, 2021}}</ref> In 1960, his piano instrumental solo, "[[Last Date (song)|Last Date]]" went to number two on the [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]] [[pop music]] chart and sold over one [[Music recording sales certifications|million copies]]. Its follow-up, "[[On the Rebound]]", topped the [[UK Singles Chart]] in 1961. As a studio musician, he became one of a cadre of elite players dubbed the [[Nashville A-Team]] and he  performed on scores of [[Hit song|hit records]].  [[File:A pianist demonstrating Floyd Cramer piano style.ogg|thumb|A pianist demonstrating Floyd Cramer's "bent-note" piano style]]

==Biography==
Cramer was born in [[Shreveport, Louisiana]], and grew up in the small town of [[Huttig, Arkansas]]. He taught himself to play the [[piano]]. After finishing high school, he returned to Shreveport, where he worked as a ragtime pianist for the [[radio show]] ''[[Louisiana Hayride]]'', with a banjo-bass-and-drums combo. His earliest recordings, on the [[Abbott Records|Abbott]] label, carry the credit "Featuring Floyd Cramer on the Piano with Louisiana Hayride Band".

After [[Elvis Presley]] performed on ''Louisiana Hayride'' in 1955, he hired his own band which included Cramer, [[Jimmy Day (musician)|Jimmy Day]], [[Scotty Moore]], [[Bill Black]], and [[D.J. Fontana]]. This group remained his supporting band for much of that year; however, when Presley asked them to relocate to Hollywood, Cramer and Day declined to follow him there, preferring to remain in Nashville to pursue independent careers as studio musicians.<ref name="ankenybio">{{cite web |last1=Ankeny |first1=Jason |title=Jimmy Day |url=https://www.allmusic.com/artist/jimmy-day-mn0000296432 |website=allmusic.com |publisher=AllMusic, Netaktion |access-date=February 28, 2021}}</ref> In [[Nashville]], Cramer found that piano accompaniment in [[country music]] was growing in popularity. By the next year he was, in his words, "in day and night doing session".<ref>[http://countrymusichalloffame.org/artists/artist-detail/floyd-cramer "Floyd Cramer"] {{Webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20150413204143/http://countrymusichalloffame.org/artists/artist-detail/floyd-cramer |date=2015-04-13 }}, Country Music Hall of Fame and Museum. Retrieved September 17, 2014.</ref> Before long, he was one of the busiest [[studio musicians]] in the industry, playing piano for stars such as [[Elvis Presley]], [[Brenda Lee]], [[Patsy Cline]], the [[the Browns|Browns]], [[Jim Reeves]], [[Eddy Arnold]], [[Roy Orbison]], [[Don Gibson]], and the [[Everly Brothers]], among others.<ref name=pc10>{{Pop Chronicles |10 |3 |Chet Atkins}}</ref> It was Cramer's piano playing, for instance, on Presley's first [[RCA Victor]] single, "[[Heartbreak Hotel]]". While Cramer was well-established as a session player, he had a long career as a solo performer with dozens of his own albums and singles, including some Top 40 instrumental hits.

Cramer had released records under his own name since the early 1950s and became well known following the release of "[[Last Date (song)|Last Date]]", a [[Gramophone record|45-rpm]] single, released by RCA Victor in 1960.<ref>"Last Date" is the closing theme of [[Ray Hadley]]'s radio show on Sydney's radio station [[2GB]].</ref> The instrumental piece exhibited a relatively new concept in piano playing known as the "slip note" style. The record went to number two on the [[Billboard Hot 100|''Billboard'' Hot 100]] [[pop music]] chart, sold over one million copies, and was awarded a [[music recording sales certification|gold disc]]. The song was kept out of the number 1 position by Elvis Presley's "[[Are You Lonesome Tonight?]]"<ref name="The Book of Golden Discs">{{cite book

In 1961, Cramer had a hit with "[[On the Rebound]]", which went to number 4, and number 1 on the [[UK Singles Chart]].<ref name="500 Number One Hits">{{cite book
| first= Jo
| last= Rice
| year= 1982

By the mid-1960s, Cramer had become a respected performer, making numerous albums and touring with guitar maestro [[Chet Atkins]] and [[saxophonist]] [[Boots Randolph]], sometimes headlining and sometimes as the opening act for [[Eddy Arnold]]. Cramer also performed with them as a member of the [[Million Dollar Band (country music group)|Million Dollar Band]].

Cramer died of [[lung cancer]] on New Year's Eve, 1997, at the age of 64. He was interred in the [[Spring Hill Cemetery (Nashville)|Spring Hill Cemetery]] in the Nashville suburb of [[Madison, Tennessee]].

==Legacy==
Cramer’s grandson, Jason Coleman, followed in his footsteps taking up the piano, performing with him on television and in concert at a young age. At age 17, he played "[[Please Help Me, I'm Falling]]", the first song to feature Cramer's signature slip notes, with [[Hank Locklin]] at the [[Grand Ole Opry]], and two years later played piano for the Medallion Ceremony at Cramer's induction into the [[Country Music Hall of Fame]]. He carries on his grandfather's legacy with recordings and a touring tribute concert, The Piano Magic of Floyd Cramer, sharing the piano arrangements and story of Cramer's contributions to American music.

==Awards==
In 2003, Cramer was inducted into the Country Music Hall of Fame and the [[Rock and Roll Hall of Fame]]. In 2004, his recording of "Last Date" was inducted into the Grammy Hall of Fame, established to honor recordings of lasting qualitative or historical significance. In 2008, Cramer was inducted into the [[Louisiana Music Hall of Fame]].

[[East Tennessee State University]], in [[Johnson City, Tennessee]], offers the Floyd Cramer Competitive Scholarship.
