'''James Michael Bonamy''' (born April 29, 1972) is an American pastor and former [[country music]] artist. He has released two studio albums (1996's ''[[What I Live to Do]]'' and 1997's ''[[Roots and Wings (James Bonamy album)|Roots and Wings]]''), both on the [[Epic Records]] Nashville label. These albums produced seven singles on the ''[[Billboard (magazine)|Billboard]]'' Hot Country Singles & Tracks (now [[Hot Country Songs]]) charts, of which the highest-peaking was "[[I Don't Think I Will]]" at number 2.

==Early life==
Bonamy was born in [[Winter Park, Florida]] in 1972.<ref name="allmusic"/> Growing up around the beach as well as around dirt car-racing tracks near his home, he played outside linebacker and fullback in high school football. Bonamy's father was of Lebanese descent.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.chicagotribune.com/news/ct-xpm-1996-09-05-9609050091-story.html|title=WITH A NO. 1 HIT, BONAMY IS BARKING UP THE RIGHT TREE|author=Jack Hurst|website=Chicagotribune.com}}</ref> He graduated from [[Mainland High School]] in [[Daytona Beach, Florida]] in 1990. After graduating in the fall of 1990, he followed his brother to the University of Alabama. He spent most of his first year sitting in the off-campus apartment he and his brother shared, playing guitar and trying to write country songs. After performing on a local radio show in [[Alabama]], he quit school to work at a gift shop in [[Orlando, Florida]].<ref>{{cite web|url=http://articles.sun-sentinel.com/1996-04-26/news/9604240195_1_church-street-station-tammy-wynette-alabama|archive-url=https://archive.today/20120712094326/http://articles.sun-sentinel.com/1996-04-26/news/9604240195_1_church-street-station-tammy-wynette-alabama|url-status=dead|archive-date=July 12, 2012|title=Florida Native James Bonamy Got Start On Alabama Radio Show|date=April 26, 1996|work=[[Orlando Sentinel]]|accessdate=April 27, 2011}}</ref> Later, he performed in a program called "Country Music USA" at the [[Opryland USA]] theme park in [[Nashville, Tennessee]].<ref name="allmusic"/> Bonamy also competed on ''[[Star Search]]''.<ref>{{cite news|url=http://nl.newsbank.com/nl-search/we/Archives?p_product=PBPB&p_theme=pbpb&p_action=search&p_maxdocs=200&p_topdoc=1&p_text_direct-0=0EAF3FACD7267493&p_field_direct-0=document_id&p_perpage=10&p_sort=YMD_date:D&s_trackval=GooglePM|title=DAYTONA'S BONAMY SERIOUS ABOUT MUSIC|last=Lomartire|first=Paul|date=October 11, 1996|newspaper=[[Palm Beach Post]]|accessdate=October 16, 2011}}</ref>

===1995–1996: ''What I Live to Do''===
In 1995, he was signed to [[Epic Records]],<ref name="allmusic"/> and his debut album, ''[[What I Live to Do]]'', was released in 1996 under the production of [[Doug Johnson (record producer)|Doug Johnson]]. The first single, "Dog on a Toolbox," peaked at number 64 on the [[Hot Country Songs]] charts, having been withdrawn as a single because label staff thought that there were too many songs about dogs at the time.<ref name="billboard">{{cite magazine|last=Flippo|first=Chet|date=April 19, 1997|title=James Bonamy Remains Dogged with New Epic Set|magazine=Billboard|pages=30, 55|url=https://books.google.com/books?id=9w4EAAAAMBAJ&dq=%22James+bonamy%22&pg=PA30}}</ref> After this song was withdrawn, its original [[b-side]], "She's Got a Mind of Her Own" was shipped as the second single.<ref name="whitburn">{{cite book|last=Whitburn|first=Joel|title=Hot Country Songs 1944 to 2008|publisher=Record Research, Inc|year=2008|page=54|isbn=978-0-89820-177-2}}</ref> This song peaked at 26 on the country music charts, followed by the number 2 hit "[[I Don't Think I Will]]" and the number 27 "All I Do Is Love Her."<ref name="whitburn"/> At the 1997 [[Academy of Country Music Awards]], Bonamy was nominated as [[Academy of Country Music Award for New Male Artist of the Year|Top New Male Vocalist]],<ref name="billboard"/> and [[The Nashville Network|TNN]] Music City News nominated him as Star of Tomorrow.<ref name="allmusic"/> In addition, he toured in 1997 with [[Emilio Navaira]], [[Clay Walker]] and [[Terri Clark]].<ref name="billboard"/>

Dan Kuchar of ''[[Country Standard Time]]'' gave the album a mixed review, praising Bonamy's voice but criticizing the songwriting on most of the tracks and saying that the album did not have a distinctive sound.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.countrystandardtime.com/d/cdreview.asp?xid=1291|title=''What I Live to Do'' review|last=Kuchar|first=Dan|work=Country Standard Time|accessdate=August 31, 2010}}</ref>

===1997: ''Roots and Wings''===
A second album, ''[[Roots and Wings (James Bonamy album)|Roots and Wings]]'', followed in 1997.<ref>{{Cite web|url=https://www.oldies.com/artist-biography/James-Bonamy.html|title=James Bonamy Biography|website=Oldies.com|accessdate=December 1, 2019}}</ref> Its lead-off single "[[The Swing (song)|The Swing]]" was his last top 40 hit at number 31; the other two singles were "Naked to the Pain" and "Little Blue Dot", reaching numbers 65 and 63 respectively.<ref name="whitburn"/> Bonamy wrote the track "I Knew I'd Need My Heart Someday" with Johnson and [[Pat Bunch]], and chose the title track (which was written by [[Skip Ewing]] and [[Bill Anderson (singer)|Bill Anderson]] and previously recorded by [[Doug Supernaw]]<ref name="billboard"/>) at the suggestion of Epic's A&R director, Debbie Zavitson.<ref name="billboard"/>

This album also received a mixed review from ''Country Standard Time'', with Larry Stevens praising "Daddy Never Had a Chance in Hell" but criticizing the rest of the album for having a more pop-oriented sound.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://www.countrystandardtime.com/d/cdreview.asp?xid=1290|title=''Roots and Wings'' review|last=Stephens|first=Larry|work=Country Standard Time|accessdate=August 31, 2010}}</ref> Thom Owens of [[Allmusic]] gave a three-star rating out of five, saying that he considered it an improvement over the debut.<ref>{{cite web|url={{AllMusic|class=album|id=r277854|pure_url=yes}}|title=''Roots and Wings'' review|last=Owens|first=Thom|work=Allmusic|accessdate=August 31, 2010}}</ref>

==Personal life==
Bonamy has not recorded an album since 1997. By 2002, he had moved to [[Cypress, Texas]], where he worked at a communications company.<ref>{{Cite web|url=http://www.cmt.com/news/1458841/hot-talk-another-tour-more-books-thanksgiving-and-lost-stars/|archive-url=https://web.archive.org/web/20170729050256/http://www.cmt.com/news/1458841/hot-talk-another-tour-more-books-thanksgiving-and-lost-stars/|url-status=dead|archive-date=July 29, 2017|title=HOT TALK: Another Tour, More Books, Thanksgiving and Lost Stars|first=Edward|last=Morris|website=Cmt.com|accessdate=December 1, 2019}}</ref> He served full time as a Worship Pastor at [[Christ Fellowship]] in [[Palm Beach Gardens, Florida]] from 2010 to 2012.<ref>{{cite web|url=http://gochristfellowship.com/events/night-of-prayer-encounter/|title=Night of Prayer Encounter|work=Christ Fellowship|accessdate=July 11, 2011}}{{Dead link|date=January 2020 |bot=InternetArchiveBot |fix-attempted=yes }}</ref> He is married to Amy Jane and has three sons, Daniel, Paul and Sal, and currently lives in Longview, Texas where he is a Pastor at his church home, Grace Creek Church.
